# Social prescribing

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Social prescribing means that healthcare professionals – doctors, nurses, psychologists, or health guides – can prescribe non-medical interventions such as community activities, nature visits, cultural experiences, or practical support. The patient receives a personal "social prescription" (social referral) to a local activity or service, often with follow-up by a link worker. The model is based on the insight that many healthcare visits are driven by social needs rather than medical ones – loneliness, lack of meaning, financial worry – and that the answer is not found in a pill but in a community. Social prescribing is already established in the UK (NHS) and is being tested in several Swedish regions. The goal is to relieve the healthcare system, reduce medicalization, and give people tools to truly improve their life situation.

## The mechanism

A large proportion of visits to primary care centers are not about specific disease symptoms, but about vague conditions like fatigue, anxiety, depression, or pain without a clear cause. Behind these symptoms often lie social factors: loneliness, stress, unemployment, relationship problems, or a lack of meaningful occupation. Prescribing antidepressants or sick leave is easy for the healthcare system, but it does not solve the root problem – and often leads to long-term dependence on healthcare contacts.

**The measure**: Social prescribing is a structured process where:

  - Healthcare professionals identify a patient whose health problems have a clear social component.
  - Together with the patient, a "social prescription" is formulated for a specific activity: e.g., a walking group, a book club, volunteer work, a gardening association, or a mindfulness course.
  - A **link worker** (social prescriber) – often a health guide, social worker, or specially trained nurse – takes over and guides the patient to the right activity, follows up, and supports them if necessary.
  - The patient participates in the activity for a period (often 8–12 weeks), after which the effect is evaluated.

**The evidence base**:

  - NHS England reports that social prescribing reduces the pressure on primary care by an average of 28% for participating patients.
  - A systematic review (BMJ Open, 2020) found a significant improvement in mental well-being, self-rated health, and social cohesion.
  - In Swedish pilots (e.g., Region Skåne), social prescribing has been linked to a reduced prescription of antidepressants and sleeping pills.

**Examples of social prescriptions**:

| Type of activity | Example | Suitable for patient with... |
|------------------|---------|---------------------------|
| Physical community | Walking group, Nordic walking, garden therapy | Mild depression, isolation, overweight |
| Cultural activities | Choir singing, painting, book club, theater group | Anxiety, stress, lack of meaning |
| Practical support | Fixer service, debt and budget counseling, digital guidance | Financial worry, practical obstacles in everyday life |
| Nature-based interventions | Nature guiding, forest bathing, allotment garden | Exhaustion, stress, concentration difficulties |
| Meaningful occupation | Volunteer work, language cafe, mentorship | Loneliness, lack of structure |

## Pilot design – 3 primary care centers in different contexts

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: Three primary care centers in Region Stockholm test the model with dedicated link workers and a local network of social activities.

| Primary care center | Area type | Number of patients (listed) | Link worker (FTE) | Partners |
|-------------|------------|---------------------------|----------------------|--------------------|
| **Rinkeby Primary Care Center** | Socio-economically vulnerable suburb | 12,000 | 1.0 | Rinkeby Folkets Hus, study associations, local associations, social services |
| **Nynäshamn Primary Care Center** | Smaller town with rural surroundings | 15,000 | 1.0 | SPF Seniorerna, Friluftsfrämjandet, the church, the municipality's leisure administration |
| **Östermalm Primary Care Center** | Wealthy inner city | 10,000 | 0.5 (to test the model in a context with different expectations) | Gyms, private training facilities, cultural institutions, volunteer center |

**The process in detail**:

1.  **Identification**: A doctor or nurse notes during a visit that the patient's problems have social causes. Example: "I am so tired and lack energy" (actually lonely and without occupation).
2.  **Consent and referral**: The patient consents to a social prescription. The healthcare professional writes a short referral in the medical record system (a template is available).
3.  **The link worker takes over**: The link worker contacts the patient within 3 days, conducts an in-depth social mapping, and together with the patient proposes one or more suitable activities.
4.  **Participation and follow-up**: The patient participates in the activity. The link worker checks in after 4, 8, and 12 weeks.
5.  **Termination or continuation**: After 12 weeks, the effect is evaluated. If the patient feels better and wants to continue on their own, the contact with the link worker is terminated. If needs remain, a new prescription can be written or other interventions tried.

**Evaluation**:

  - Number of social prescriptions per 1,000 listed patients and year.
  - Proportion of patients who complete the social activity (at least 8 out of 12 weeks).
  - Change in self-rated health (EQ-5D) and well-being (WHO-5) after 12 weeks.
  - Change in the number of healthcare visits (primary care, emergency room) 6 months before and 6 months after the intervention.
  - Cost per social prescription compared to the average cost of healthcare visits and medication.

**Financing**:

  - **Region Stockholm**: Finances the link workers' salaries as part of primary care's transition to "Nära vård" (Close care).
  - **Municipal co-financing**: The municipalities have a vested interest in reducing social services' costs for loneliness and mental illness. Can contribute with premises and activities.
  - **Research funds**: Forte, Vinnova, or Allmänna arvsfonden (The Swedish Inheritance Fund) can finance the evaluation.

**Note**: The cost of social prescribing is low compared to the alternative. A single emergency visit costs \~SEK 5,000. A sick leave costs society \~SEK 100,000 per year. The model quickly becomes cost-neutral or profitable.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **The Social Services Act (SoL)**: Clarify that municipalities may collaborate with regions on social prescribing and that prescribed activities can be financed jointly.
2.  **The Health and Medical Services Act (HSL)**: Task the regions with integrating social prescribing as part of primary care's core mission. Clarify that a social prescription is a valid measure in the same way as a medical prescription.
3.  **The Patient Data Act**: Enable information transfer between the primary care center and the link worker (who is often employed by the municipality or a non-profit organization) with the patient's consent. A simple consent template is developed nationally.
4.  **The Pharmaceutical Benefits Act**: Not directly relevant, but a principled statement that a "social activity on prescription" is equated with medication in health economic calculations.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is not the task of healthcare – we should diagnose and treat disease, not organize book clubs."

**Answer**: If a patient comes to the primary care center because they are lonely and depressed, it is healthcare's responsibility to offer the most effective help. Often, this is not a drug but a social context. To ignore this is to fail the patient and waste tax funds.

### "Is there evidence that this works in Sweden?"

**Answer**: There are now several Swedish pilots: Region Skåne has run social prescribing since 2019 with positive results. Region Stockholm has tested "Culture on prescription". The evaluations show improved well-being and reduced healthcare consumption. We need to scale up, not reinvent the wheel.

### "There are no activities to prescribe to, especially not in rural areas."

**Answer**: On the contrary. Sweden has a rich associational life, study associations, churches, and non-profit organizations in almost every municipality. The challenge is to make the offerings visible and coordinate them. The link worker's task is precisely to map and build relationships with local actors.

### "Patients will not want to be 'prescribed' a walking group – it feels stigmatizing."

**Answer**: Experiences from the UK show that patients appreciate being taken seriously and offered an alternative to pills. Social prescribing is based on voluntariness and the patient's own motivation. The link worker's role is to listen and guide, not to force.

### "How do we know the patient actually goes there? What if they just stay at home?"

**Answer**: The link worker follows up and supports. If the patient does not go, the reasons are explored together – perhaps the wrong activity was chosen, or there are practical obstacles. Flexibility and individual adaptation are key.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Region Skåne's "Social prescribing"**: Pilot at several primary care centers since 2019. Evaluation by Lund University shows a reduced number of healthcare visits and improved self-rated health.
  - **Culture on prescription in Region Stockholm**: Tested in, among other places, Järfälla and on Gotland. Participants have attended theater, concerts, and museums with follow-up conversations.
  - **Nature on prescription**: Being tested in the Västra Götaland Region, where patients receive guided nature visits as a complement to traditional treatment for exhaustion and stress.
  - **Fixer services (Fixartjänster)**: Available in over 100 municipalities. Seniors get help with simple practical chores – a form of social prescribing that reduces fall accidents and isolation.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a primary care center manager**: Start by mapping local resources in the immediate area – what associations, meeting places, and activities are within walking distance? Create a simple list that staff can hand out.

**If you are a regional politician**: Submit a motion that social prescribing should be included in the region's action plan for "Nära vård" (Close care). Allocate funds to train link workers and build local networks.

**If you work in social services**: Take the initiative to collaborate with nearby primary care centers. Offer your meeting places and activities as a resource for social prescribing.

**If you are involved in an association**: Contact your local primary care center and tell them about your activities. Offer to receive participants via social prescription. The more activities available, the more effective the model becomes.
