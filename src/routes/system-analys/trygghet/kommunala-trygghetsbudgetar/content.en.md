# Municipal security budgets

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Every municipality receives SEK 100 per resident annually for security initiatives decided through participatory budgeting in the most vulnerable areas. The residents themselves prioritize between lighting, youth centers, security rings, mediators, de-escalation training, camera surveillance, or other local ideas. The goal: shift resources from reactive policing to preventive measures that actually create security – and let those who experience the insecurity decide what helps.

## The mechanism

Today, security resources are governed from the top down. The police decide where patrols should go, the municipality decides on lighting, the state decides on grants for youth centers. Residents in vulnerable areas have almost no influence – even though they know best where the shoe pinches.

**The measure**: Introduce **municipal security budgets** with two components:

1.  **A base budget** of SEK 100 per resident in the entire municipality (including all areas). This finances general security measures that benefit everyone – better lighting, park maintenance, public toilets, etc.

2.  **A targeted budget** of an additional SEK 100 per resident in the areas classified as "vulnerable" or "particularly vulnerable" (according to the police list, or locally defined). This budget is distributed through **participatory budgeting**: the residents in the area vote on how the money should be used.

**Participatory budgeting in practice**:

  - Every year in September, residents in the area gather for an idea market. Everyone who lives or works in the area (including children and youth) can submit proposals.
  - The proposals are grouped into themes (lighting, youth activities, social interventions, physical environment) and cost-calculated by the municipality.
  - In November, every resident (from 16 years of age, or from 13 years with parental permission) receives one vote per person. Voting takes place digitally or on-site in a library, school, or community center.
  - The 5–10 proposals with the most votes are implemented during the coming year.
  - A council of residents (drawn by lot) follows up to ensure that the money has been used as promised.

**What the money can be used for** (examples from other participatory budgets in the world):

| Category | Examples of measures |
|----------|----------------------|
| Physical environment | Better lighting, tidying up parks, rubbish removal, bicycle stands, benches |
| Youth | Youth centers, night walks, summer jobs, sports activities, music studio |
| Social measures | Security rings, community mediators, neighborhood watch, elderly contact |
| Security | Camera surveillance (where residents want it), security walks, de-escalation training |
| Communication | Security app, information campaigns, neighborhood meetings |

**The difference compared to today's system**:

| Aspect | Today | Security budget |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| Who decides | Police, municipal officials, politicians | The residents themselves (through voting) |
| Resources | Earmarked, often reactive (police patrols) | Preventive, flexible |
| Transparency | Low (budgets are complex) | High (residents see directly where the money goes) |
| Accountability | Via elections every four years (impersonal) | Direct (council follows up, new voting every year) |

## Pilot design – 20 municipalities first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑3)**: 20 municipalities of varying sizes and geography test security budgets in 1–3 vulnerable areas per municipality.

**Selection criteria**:

  - The municipality has at least one area classified as "vulnerable" or "particularly vulnerable"
  - The municipality shows political will (decision in the municipal council) and administrative capacity
  - Geographical spread: large cities (Malmö, Gothenburg, Stockholm), medium-sized cities (Norrköping, Örebro, Gävle), smaller municipalities with vulnerable areas (Borlänge, Landskrona, Hässleholm)

**Budget model**:

| Type | Per resident | Example municipality 100,000 res. | Example vulnerable area 5,000 res. |
|------|--------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Base budget (entire municipality) | SEK 100 | SEK 10 million | – |
| Targeted budget (vulnerable areas) | SEK 100 | – | SEK 500,000 per area |

Total for a municipality like Malmö (350,000 res., approx. 100,000 in vulnerable areas): SEK 35 million (base) + SEK 10 million (targeted) = SEK 45 million/year.

**Organizational model**:

  - The municipality allocates the funds in its budget and transfers them to a **security fund** managed by an independent steering group (50% residents, 25% municipality, 25% civil society).
  - A **security guide** (1 per area, 0.5 FTE) is responsible for supporting the participatory budgeting process – informing, collecting proposals, organizing voting, following up.
  - The voting result is **binding** for the municipality – politicians cannot override the residents' decision. Exception: proposals that violate the law (e.g., buying weapons) or are obviously impossible to implement.

**Evaluation**:

  - Participation rate: proportion of residents who submit proposals (target: \>5%) and who vote (target: \>30%)
  - Implemented measures: proportion of the budget actually used as decided (target: \>90%)
  - Security effect: change in survey responses ("do you feel safe outdoors after dark?") compared to control areas
  - Trust effect: change in trust in the municipality and the police

## Budget and financing

| Item | Annual cost (20 pilot municipalities) |
|------|---------------------------------------|
| Base budget (20 municipalities × average 50,000 res. × SEK 100) | SEK 100 million |
| Targeted budget (20 municipalities × average 2 vulnerable areas × 5,000 res. × SEK 100) | SEK 20 million |
| Security guides (40 areas × 0.5 FTE × SEK 500,000) | SEK 10 million |
| Administration, evaluation, platform | SEK 5 million |
| **Total first year** | **SEK 135 million** |

**Financing**: Compared to the police budget (SEK 35 billion) or the state's grants to municipalities for security (several billion), SEK 135 million is a very small sum. The funds can be reallocated from existing appropriations for crime prevention work (Brå, National Board of Health and Welfare, the police's preventive funds). Socio-economically: if the measures reduce crime in the pilot areas by just 5%, the reform is self-financing.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Local Government Act (2017:725)** – add a section: *"The municipality may introduce participatory budgeting for security measures in particularly vulnerable areas. Decisions made through participatory budgeting shall be binding on the municipality, provided they fit within the budget and do not violate the law."*

2.  **Ordinance on government grants for security measures** – reallocation of existing funds so that municipalities introducing security budgets are prioritized.

3.  **The Secrecy Act** – for security guides to be able to handle personal data about participants (voting lists, etc.), clarification is required in the Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act (OSL). Normally these are municipal tasks – no major problem.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "Residents cannot prioritize – they will choose populist proposals that do not work."

**Answer**: Experiences from participatory budgeting in Paris, New York, and Porto Alegre show that residents prioritize wisely – often better than politicians. They choose schools, lighting, parks – not unnecessary projects. And even if a proposal is bad, one learns from mistakes. The point is to shift power, not to guarantee perfect decisions.

### "This is expensive – SEK 100 per resident is a lot of money."

**Answer**: SEK 100 per resident is 0.1% of an average municipal budget (approx. SEK 100,000 per resident). It is less than what the municipality spends on information campaigns or anniversary celebrations. And compared to the costs of crime (SEK 200+ billion/year nationally), it is a negligible investment.

### "Politicians do not want to give up control over the budget."

**Answer**: Then they will have to answer for why they do not trust their residents. Ultimately, it is about democracy – either you believe people can decide for themselves, or you don't. Security budgets are a concrete measure to reduce the gap between politicians and citizens.

### "What happens if residents vote for camera surveillance that violates privacy?"

**Answer**: The proposal must be legal. Camera surveillance is permitted under certain conditions (crime prevention purpose, signage, privacy impact assessment). If residents vote for it – and it complies with the law – then it is a democratic decision. Better that the residents themselves choose surveillance than that the police or politicians impose it on them.

### "Are there any Swedish examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, several municipalities have tested participatory budgeting on a small scale, e.g., Uppsala, Gothenburg ("framtidens Göteborg"), Stockholm (suburban projects). But no one has done it specifically for security in vulnerable areas with binding decisions. The pilot would make Sweden a pioneer.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Participatory budgeting in Uppsala** – in 2019, Uppsala tested districts with SEK 1 million per area. The experiences were positive (high participation, satisfied residents). The scale model works.
  - **Security surveys (Brå, SKR)** – municipalities already measure security today. The same data can be used for evaluation.
  - **Municipal security councils** – exist in many municipalities, but are dominated by police and officials. Security budgets shift power to the residents.
  - **Denmark's "tryghedsbudgetter" (security budgets)** – Denmark has tested similar concepts in vulnerable residential areas with good results (fewer reported crimes, higher security). Sweden can emulate this.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal politician**: Submit a budget request to allocate SEK 100 per resident for participatory budgeting in a vulnerable area. Use the text above as a basis. Promise that you will respect the result – even if you do not agree.

**If you are a municipal official**: Propose to your manager that you carry out a smaller pilot with SEK 200,000 in a residential area. Let the residents vote between three proposals (e.g., lighting, youth center, security ring). Evaluate after one year.

**If you are a resident in a vulnerable area**: Gather your neighbors. Write a letter to the municipal executive board: "We want a security budget of SEK 100 per person – and we want to decide for ourselves how the money is used." The more people who sign, the harder it is for politicians to say no.
