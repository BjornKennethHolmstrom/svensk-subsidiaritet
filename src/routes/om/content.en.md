# About Swedish Subsidiarity

**Swedish Subsidiarity** is an independent research and policy initiative examining how centralization creates fragility in Swedish society — and what the alternatives might be.

## Background

My name is Björn Kenneth Holmström. This project emerged from an analysis of global systemic risks that led to a simple conclusion: complex problems cannot be solved with centralized control. You have to distribute intelligence.

Sweden has built remarkably efficient centralized systems. But efficiency and resilience are not the same — and resilience is what we lack.

## What I Do

- **Analyze** structural vulnerabilities in critical systems (food, healthcare, energy, preparedness)
- **Design** concrete proposals for distributed governance — what I call *närstyre* (proximity governance)
- **Develop** digital tools that can make subsidiarity practically achievable

This is also the foundation for **Global Governance Frameworks (GGF)**, a broader framework with Sweden as the first application.

## An Honest Disclaimer

This is a one-person initiative using AI as a tool. I have no organization behind me, no party support, no funding. What I have is systems thinking, a technical background, and a conviction that someone needs to ask these questions — even if the answers take time.

## The Larger Context

My engagement with distributed systems is not isolated. It is part of a broader exploration of how human development, consciousness, and societal structures interconnect. For those interested, I also run:

- **[Spiralize.org](https://spiralize.org)** — a resource on Spiral Dynamics and values development, which helps understand *why* systems thinking and subsidiarity are becoming relevant at this point in human development.
- **[Nondualize.org](https://nondualize.org)** — a space for exploring non-dual awareness, a reminder that beyond all systems and structures lies simple being.

These projects are separate from Swedish Subsidiarity, but they share a common thread: a conviction that real change requires both inner and outer transformation.
