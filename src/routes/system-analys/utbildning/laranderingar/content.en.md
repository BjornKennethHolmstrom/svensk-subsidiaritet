# Learning rings

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Networks of competence-sharing groups (called learning rings) that connect students with expertise in the local community—craftspeople, entrepreneurs, retired engineers, artists, artisan farmers, cultural practitioners. Each learning ring consists of 10–20 students and 3–5 "experts" (knowledgeable individuals) from the community. The group meets 2–4 hours a week, often at a workplace, association premises, or in nature. The goal: to break the monopoly of the standardized curriculum by adding local, practical, and context-bound knowledge that the school cannot offer today. Financing is done by redirecting existing association and leisure grants (about SEK 1,000 per child and year)—no new taxes or large state grants are required.

## The mechanism

Today, the school is a closed institution. Students spend thousands of hours in classrooms but rarely encounter the knowledge that exists outside the school walls—how to start a business, repair a car, grow food, build furniture, lead an association, preserve a cultural tradition. At the same time, there is an enormous untapped resource in society: skilled professionals, experienced seniors, newly arrived immigrants with unique skills, entrepreneurs, cultural workers.

**The measure**: A **learning ring** is a structured but flexible group where:

  - 10–20 students (usually from the same school or grade) sign up voluntarily.
  - 3–5 "experts" (community experts) are recruited from the local community—a retired civil engineer, a florist, a game designer, a blacksmith, a nurse.
  - The group meets during a semester (8–12 sessions) to work on a practical project: building a birdhouse, starting a mini-company, documenting local history, growing vegetables, creating a podcast.
  - Each session is led by a teacher or youth recreation leader together with one or more of the experts.
  - The students document their learning in a **portfolio** (images, reflections, finished products) that can be used for assessment in school (replacing some traditional teaching).

**What the learning ring adds** (that the school lacks):

| Needs in today's school | The learning ring's solution |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Withdrawal from practical knowledge | Tools, materials, work steps – learning by doing |
| Lack of local role models | Young people meet adults with real professions and passions |
| Hard to motivate theoretical subjects | Math through carpentry, Swedish through podcasts, biology through farming |
| Standardized curriculum doesn't suit everyone | Local knowledge becomes part of the teaching |

**The financing model** (the key innovation):

Today, every child in Sweden receives about SEK 1,000 a year in various forms of association grants, leisure grants, culture checks, and municipal activity support. The money is fragmented and often used inefficiently. The proposal: **Redirect SEK 500 per child and year** from these pots to a **learning ring check** that the school can use to pay the experts (fee SEK 200–500 per session), materials, snacks (fika), and venue rental.

This is how the flow looks:

1.  The municipality gathers existing funds from various grants (association grants, culture checks, leisure funds) into a common **learning ring pot**.
2.  Schools apply for funds to start learning rings (simple digital application, max 5 minutes).
3.  Granted learning rings receive a check of SEK 5,000–15,000 per semester (depending on the number of students and sessions).
4.  The school uses the money for fees to the experts (SEK 300/hour is a good level), materials, and refreshments.
5.  Accounting is done through the portfolio (what the students have learned)—not through bureaucratic receipts.

## Pilot design – 50 learning rings first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑3)**: 50 learning rings are started in 10–20 municipalities with varying profiles—large city, rural area, high proportion of newly arrived immigrants, high proportion of elderly. Each learning ring comprises 15 students on average.

**Selection criteria**:

  - The municipality commits to gathering existing grants into a learning ring pot (at least SEK 500 per student in the municipality).
  - The school appoints a **learning ring coordinator** (teacher or recreation leader) who recruits experts and organizes the meetings.
  - The learning ring must be free for the students (no fee) and available to everyone regardless of socio-economic background.

**Examples of learning rings in the pilot**:

| Location | Theme | Experts | Project |
|-------|------|---------|---------|
| Kiruna | Mining technology and Sami handicraft | Retired mining engineer, Sami duodji artisan | Build a model of the mine, make a pewter piece of jewelry |
| Gotland | Sustainable tourism and Middle Ages | Guide at the Visby city wall, hotel manager, archaeologist | Plan a virtual tour, interview tourists |
| Rosengård (Malmö) | Entrepreneurship and cooking | Restaurateur with an immigrant background, e-commerce entrepreneur | Start a mini-cafe, cook dishes from different countries |
| Värmland countryside | Forestry and handicraft | Hunter, artisan, carpenter | Build birdhouses, make a stool, learn to chop wood |
| Norrköping | Tech and design | Game developer, graphic designer, programmer | Create a simple computer game, design a logo |

**Evaluation**:

  - Number of participating students (target: 750 the first year, 2,000 year 3)
  - Students' motivation and attendance (compared to regular teaching)
  - Learning effect: measurement of specific skills (e.g., math through building projects) compared to a control group
  - Teachers' experience of workload and meaningfulness
  - Change in collaboration between school and local community

## Budget and financing

| Item | Amount (per learning ring 15 students, 10 meetings) | Total 50 learning rings |
|------|-------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Fee to experts (3 people × 10 meetings × SEK 300) | SEK 9,000 | SEK 450,000 |
| Materials (wood, tools, food, printing) | SEK 3,000 | SEK 150,000 |
| Fika, venue rental (if needed) | SEK 2,000 | SEK 100,000 |
| Learning ring coordinator's time (10 hours planning) | SEK 5,000 (overtime/comp) | SEK 250,000 |
| **Total per learning ring** | **SEK 19,000** | **SEK 950,000** |

**Financing**: SEK 950,000 for 750 students = approximately SEK 1,270 per student. This is in line with current association and leisure grants (approx. SEK 1,000–1,500 per child). By redirecting existing funds (no new tax), the pilot can be financed entirely within the municipalities' current budgets. For the state: a minor reallocation within the Swedish National Agency for Education's and culture school funds can cover national coordination.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Education Act (2010:800)** – add a section: *"The school may, as a complement to regular teaching, organize learning rings where students carry out practical projects under the guidance of teachers and external knowledge bearers. The learning ring can replace up to 20% of the teaching time in a subject if the goals in the curriculum are met."*

2.  **Ordinance on association grants** – municipalities are given the right to merge different grant pots into a learning ring pot without violating earmarking rules.

3.  **Exception to the Secrecy Act** – the experts do not need to undergo formal teacher certification, but must be able to show an extract from the criminal record (to protect the students). This is regulated in a simple ordinance.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is handing teaching over to amateurs – teachers are educated for a reason."

**Answer**: The learning ring is a complement, not a replacement. The teacher is always present and is responsible for connecting the project to the curriculum's goals. The experts contribute their unique expertise – just as the school currently invites guest lecturers. The difference is that it becomes structured and recurring.

### "Trade unions will protest – this threatens teachers' jobs."

**Answer**: On the contrary. Teachers get a more varied workday and can focus on what they are good at: planning, leading, and assessing learning. The experts relieve the burden by providing practical guidance. Several teachers' unions have expressed frustration over the lack of practical elements – the learning ring is a solution, not a threat.

### "Municipalities do not have the resources to coordinate this."

**Answer**: The model is based on reallocation, not new money. And the pilot is small – 50 learning rings require minimal administration. A person at 25% can coordinate an entire municipality. Experiences from municipalities that have already tested similar concepts (e.g., "School-Business Life") show that it works.

### "How do you guarantee that the students learn the right things?"

**Answer**: The teacher is responsible for connecting the project to the curriculum. The portfolio documents what has been learned. And if the learning ring doesn't work – then it is terminated after the semester. No coercion. The risk is minimal compared to the potential: increased motivation, deeper learning, stronger ties between school and society.

### "Are there Swedish examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, several. "School-Business Life" (Swedish Association of Local Authorities and Regions) connects schools with local companies, but it is usually single days. "Workplace Learning" (GY11) involves internships, but rarely with structured knowledge transfers. "Hela Sverige ska leva" (All Sweden Shall Live) runs community center projects with schools. The learning ring gathers these good ideas into a simple, scalable model.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Apprenticeship system (vocational programs)** – already exists, but lacks an equivalent for theoretical programs. The learning ring is a lightweight version.
  - **Creative School (Skapande skola)** – state grant for culture in school, but often one-off visits. The learning ring is recurring and integrated.
  - **After-school centers' makerspaces** – some after-school centers have workshops, but rarely linked to subject teaching. The learning ring can be integrated.
  - **Norway's "The Cultural Schoolbag" (Den kulturelle skolesekken)** – a similar model where artists and artisans work with schools over time. Sweden can copy and broaden this to all types of knowledge bearers.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a teacher or principal**: Call a retired professional in your area—a carpenter, a gardener, an entrepreneur—and ask: "Can you come and hold a workshop for my class once a week for a month?" Start on a small scale.

**If you are a municipal politician**: Submit a proposal to allocate SEK 100,000 for a pilot with 5 learning rings in your municipality. Use the text above as a basis. Show that the money can be taken from existing association grants.

**If you are a professional (retiree, craftsperson, artist, engineer)**: Contact your local school. Tell them what you can teach. Offer to be an "expert" in a learning ring. Even a few hours a month makes a big difference.
