# Energy cooperatives

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Local energy cooperatives that own and operate renewable electricity production—wind power, solar parks, small-scale hydropower, or biogas. The members (households, companies, municipalities) are co-owners, receive a share of the surplus, and have priority for the produced electricity at cost price. The model is taken from Denmark (Samsø, Middelgrunden) and Germany (over 1,000 energy cooperatives). The goal: to break Vattenfall's monopoly, increase local acceptance of green energy, and let those who bear the consequences of energy production also share the profits. Financing through green bonds, state start-up loans, and member contributions.

## The mechanism

Today, Swedish electricity production is largely owned by the state (Vattenfall), large power companies (Uniper, Eon), and venture capital-owned wind power companies. Local communities see the wind turbines, power lines, and transformer stations—but receive no share of the revenue. The result: protests, appeals, and a drawn-out permitting process that delays the green transition by several years.

**The measure**: An **energy cooperative** is an economic association (cooperative) that:

  - Is formed by at least 10 members—households, companies, organizations, or the municipality.
  - Develops, finances, and operates one or more facilities for renewable electricity production (solar, wind, water, biogas).
  - The members subscribe to shares (share capital) of, for example, SEK 10,000–50,000. The return comes from the sale of electricity (at market price or directly to the members).
  - The cooperative can sell electricity on the open market (Nord Pool) or sign agreements directly with the members ("electricity price guaranteed at cost").
  - The surplus (profit) is distributed to the members as interest on the share capital, or reinvested in new projects.

**Advantages compared to large-scale commercial projects**:

| Aspect | Commercial company | Energy cooperative |
|--------|-------------------|------------------|
| Owner | Venture capital, large corporations | Local members (households, companies) |
| Profit motive | Maximize profit to external owners | Back to members, often lower return requirements |
| Local acceptance | Low (external actors) | High (members are neighbors) |
| Permitting process | 5–10 years, many appeals | 2–4 years (if local acceptance exists) |
| Cost of capital | 6–8% (venture capital) | 3–4% (green bonds, member contributions, state loans) |
| Local economy | Money leaves the district | Money stays locally (interest, jobs, services) |

**Practical example (hypothetical Swedish case)**:

*A group of 50 households in a village in Västerbotten forms a cooperative. They join forces with the local community association and a small sawmill. They plan four wind turbines (total capacity 12 MW) on a nearby ridge. The cooperative raises member contributions totaling SEK 5 million, a state start-up loan of SEK 10 million (interest-free for 3 years), and a green bond loan of SEK 80 million from Kommuninvest or the European Investment Bank. The facility generates an annual turnover of approx. SEK 15 million (with normal wind). After interest and operations, the profit is SEK 4 million per year—which is divided between the members (approx. SEK 80,000 per household per year) and reinvestments. The district also gets several local jobs (operations, maintenance, administration).*

## Pilot design – 10 cooperatives first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑5)**: Ten energy cooperatives in different parts of the country receive state start-up support and advice. The selection is made via an application round to the Swedish Energy Agency (Energimyndigheten).

**Proposed candidates** (realistic based on geography and local engagement):

| Location | Type | Members | Facility | Estimated capacity |
|-------|-----|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| Piteå | Solar park + battery | 100 households, the municipality | 5 hectares of land | 3 MW solar + 2 MWh battery |
| Öland | Wind power | 150 households, farms | 4 turbines (2 MW each) | 8 MW |
| Varberg | Offshore wind | 200 households, local companies | 6 turbines (3 MW) | 18 MW (pilot) |
| Borås | Biogas (farm-based) | 50 farmers | Digestion plant | 5 MW heat + electricity |
| Åre | Small-scale hydropower | 50 households, tourism companies | 2 existing mills renovated | 1 MW |
| Skåne (Tomelilla) | Solar cell association | 300 homeowners | 15 hectares of arable land | 10 MW solar |
| Dalarna (Leksand) | Mixed (solar+biogas) | Community association, municipality | Solar cells on roofs, farm biogas | 2 MW electricity + heat |
| Västerbotten (Lycksele) | Wind power | Sami, farmers, villagers | 3 turbines (3.6 MW) | 10.8 MW |
| Gotland | Solar + battery storage | 500 households (tourism industry) | 20 hectares of land, battery park | 15 MW solar + 10 MWh battery |
| Närke (Kumla) | Bioenergy (wood chips) | Several farms, district heating company | Wood chip boiler, ORC turbine | 5 MW electricity, 20 MW heat |

**Steps to form a cooperative in the pilot**:

1.  **Interest group**: At least 10 people/companies sign a letter of intent.
2.  **Feasibility study**: The cooperative receives a grant of SEK 100,000 (from the Swedish Energy Agency) to investigate technical, economic, and legal prerequisites.
3.  **Capital raising**: The cooperative subscribes shares among its members. The state offers co-financing (start-up loan) up to 50% of the project cost, max SEK 10 million per cooperative. Repayment with interest (1–2%) after 5 years.
4.  **Permits**: The cooperative applies for grid connection, environmental permits, and building permits. Pilot cooperatives receive priority in processing (max 12 months) and exemptions from certain requirements (e.g., conducting a full Environmental Impact Assessment if the capacity is below a certain level).
5.  **Construction**: The cooperative procures contracting (can be local). Completion within 2 years.
6.  **Operations**: Electricity is sold on Nord Pool or directly to members via agreements. The surplus is distributed.

**Evaluation**:

  - Number of members per cooperative (target: `>`100)
  - Average return on member contribution (target: 4–6% per year)
  - Time from idea to operation (target: `<`4 years, compared to 8–10 years for commercial wind farms)
  - Local acceptance (survey to residents in the area before/after)
  - Change in the number of appeals (expected close to zero)

## Budget and financing

| Item | Amount (per pilot cooperative) | Total 10 cooperatives |
|------|------------------------------|----------------------|
| Feasibility study grant (SEK 100,000) | SEK 0.1 million | SEK 1 million |
| State start-up loan (max SEK 10 million, repaid) | SEK 10 million on average | SEK 100 million (loan) |
| Interest subsidy (1% for 3 years) | SEK 0.3 million (per loan) | SEK 3 million (cost) |
| Advice (Swedish Energy Agency, cooperative center) | SEK 200,000 | SEK 2 million |
| Evaluation | SEK 100,000 | SEK 1 million |
| **Total state cost** (excl. loans to be repaid) | **SEK 0.7 million per cooperative** | **SEK 7 million** |

**Financing**: SEK 7 million in direct costs (grants, interest subsidies, advice) is a very small investment compared to the socio-economic benefit. If each cooperative builds facilities for SEK 50–100 million (private and borrowed funds), an investment volume of SEK 500–1,000 million is created with minimal state input. Furthermore, appeals and lead times are reduced, which speeds up the green transition.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Electricity Act (1997:857)** – make it easier for small-scale producers to sell electricity directly to members ("direct agreements"). Today, the cooperative is required to register as an electricity trading company (heavy and expensive). Introduce an exemption for cooperatives under 50 GWh/year.
2.  **Amendment to the Environmental Code (1998:808)** – introduce a simplified permitting process for renewable electricity production owned by local cooperatives (e.g., abolished EIA for wind power under 10 MW, if the cooperative and nearby residents are members). Consultation requirements remain, but the right to appeal is limited to members.
3.  **Ordinance on state start-up loans** – the Swedish Energy Agency is tasked with establishing a loan guarantee for energy cooperatives, with 1–2% interest and an interest-only period for 3 years.
4.  **Tax relief** – cooperatives that produce electricity for direct consumption among their members are exempted from energy tax (just as private solar electricity is already tax-free). Reduce administration.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "Vattenfall and the large power companies will lobby against it—they don't want competition."

**Answer**: Vattenfall is owned by the Swedish state. The state can instruct Vattenfall to support rather than oppose cooperatives, for example by offering technical services (grid connection, balance responsibility) at a reasonable cost. Cooperatives constitute a very small share of the market (max 5–10% in the long term)—the large companies will not lose noticeably, but they can gain from higher acceptance of wind power (which reduces the risk of future halts).

### "This will just be for rich homeowners—those who have capital to invest."

**Answer**: Cooperatives can be designed with low contributions (e.g., SEK 1,000 per share) and member loans (e.g., tenants can join without a contribution, paying a higher electricity fee instead). The municipality can be a member and subsidize shares for low-income households. Experiences from Germany show that energy cooperatives recruit broadly, including pensioners and young people. The pilot budget includes funds for advice that can be directed toward broadened participation.

### "The permitting process is already long—an exemption for cooperatives creates unequal treatment."

**Answer**: The point is exactly to correct a distortion. Commercial companies can handle long processes (they have capital and lawyers). Cooperatives do not have the same resources. A simplified procedure for local, member-owned projects is proportionate and increases the net amount of green energy—because otherwise they would not be built at all.

### "Won't the grid owners (Eon, Vattenfall Eldistribution) hinder connection?"

**Answer**: The grid owner is obliged to connect all approved production facilities according to the Electricity Act. Cooperatives can appeal unjustified obstacles to the Swedish Energy Markets Inspectorate (Ei). The pilot's legal support includes help with such disputes. Experiences from other countries (Germany, Denmark) show that grid owners cooperate when there are political directives.

### "Are there any Swedish examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, some small-scale ones. **Kraftringen** in Lund (municipally owned, not cooperative). **The Oxhagsverket wind power cooperative** in Skåne (formed by villagers, around since the 1990s). **Southern Sweden's Energy Cooperative** (smaller). But no major spread. Denmark has over 100,000 households in wind cooperatives. Germany has 1,000+ energy cooperatives. Sweden is lagging behind—the pilot can start a popular movement.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Oxhagsverket (Skåne)** – Sweden's oldest wind cooperative, built in 1991, still in operation. Members are local farmers and households. The model works.
  - **Lotsbroverket (Värmdö)** – smaller cooperatively owned wind turbine. Inspired by Denmark.
  - **Bygdealiansen** (Association for building communities and cooperatives) – can support start-ups.
  - **Denmark's Samsø** – world-famous example of an island that became 100% renewable through cooperatives. A Swedish pilot can invite Samsø's experts as advisors.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a farmer or landowner**: Gather your neighbors. Join forces with the local community association. Invite a speaker from a Danish energy cooperative. See if there is wind or solar potential on your land. Contact the Swedish Energy Agency for a feasibility study grant.

**If you are a municipal politician**: Develop a policy that prioritizes cooperatives in land allocation for wind power and solar parks. Allocate land to cooperatives at a lower lease fee. Participate in subscribing shares in the cooperative as a municipality.

**If you are a homeowner**: Investigate whether your area has a solar cooperative. If not—start one. It does not need to be large-scale: 10 neighbors jointly buying a solar park of a few hectares is a cooperative.
