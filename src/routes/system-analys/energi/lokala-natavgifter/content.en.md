# Local grid fees

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: The electricity grid fee (the fixed and variable cost that every electricity customer pays to their local grid company) is determined locally by a user-cooperative board instead of by the grid monopolist. The model entails customers in a geographical area (e.g., a municipality or a city district) joining forces and forming a cooperative that takes over the management of the local low-voltage grid (the one on the street outside the house). The cooperative leases the grid from the large grid owner (Eon, Vattenfall, Ellevio) at cost price and is itself responsible for operation, maintenance, and fee setting. The goal: to lower grid fees by eliminating profit requirements and administrative overhead, increase local transparency and control, and provide incentives for energy efficiency and flexibility. The model is taken from "municipal utility districts" in the USA and cooperative grids in Germany (Schleswig-Holstein).

## The mechanism

Today, electricity grid companies are natural monopolies – the customer cannot choose a grid owner. Each grid owner (Eon, Vattenfall Eldistribution, Ellevio, and about 40 smaller municipal companies) sets its own fees within the framework approved by the Swedish Energy Markets Inspectorate (Ei). The fees vary greatly: from SEK 0.50/kWh in some municipal grids to over SEK 1/kWh in metropolitan grids. The difference is primarily due to different profitability requirements (private grids have higher return requirements), administrative complexity, and investment needs. The customer has no influence – neither over the size of the fee nor over how the grid is managed.

**The measure**: A **cooperative grid model** where:

  - A local cooperative (user cooperative) is formed by at least 500 electricity customers (households, companies, municipal operations) within the same low-voltage area (connected to the same transformer station).
  - The cooperative negotiates with the large grid owner to **lease** the local grid (cables, transformers, service lines). The lease fee is based on the grid owner's actual capital cost (depreciation) and maintenance cost – no profit margin.
  - The cooperative takes over operation, maintenance, metering, and billing. It can hire contractors (e.g., the same as before) or do parts itself.
  - The cooperative sets the grid fee (fixed fee per month + variable fee per kWh) according to what is required to cover:
      - The lease fee to the large grid owner
      - Own operating costs (personnel, maintenance, administration)
      - Provision for future investments (new cables, transformers)
      - Any surplus (refunded to members or invested in energy efficiency)
  - The cooperative is governed by a board elected by the members (one member, one vote). All decisions regarding fees, investments, and operations are public.

**Difference compared to today's model**:

| Aspect | Today's grid company (Eon, Vattenfall etc.) | Cooperative grid |
|--------|----------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Owner | Private or municipal limited companies | The members (the customers) |
| Profit requirement | Yes (often 6–8% return on capital) | No (non-profit-maximizing) |
| Administrative overhead | High (group management, marketing, lawyers) | Low (only local operation) |
| Fee setting | Determined by Ei (based on the company's proposal) | Determined by the members at the annual meeting |
| Transparency | Low (annual report public, but complex) | High (all board decisions, members can attend) |
| Flexibility | Low (standardized fees, few exceptions) | High (differentiation between customers, discount for flexibility) |

**Potential savings**: A cooperative grid fee can be 20–40% lower than today's private grids, as profit requirements and corporate overhead disappear. For an average homeowner (approx. 20,000 kWh/year), this means a SEK 1,000–2,000 lower grid cost per year.

## Pilot design – 3 cooperative grids first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 2-5)**: Three areas (rural, suburb, smaller town) form cooperatives and lease the local grid from the large grid owner. The government enables the pilot via an exemption from the Electricity Act.

**Proposed pilots**:

| Location | Type | Number of customers | Current grid owner | Expected savings |
|-------|-----|--------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| **Hammarby Sjöstad (Stockholm)** | Urban area, mixed | 2,000 households + companies | Ellevio | 15–20% |
| **Östergötland (rural, Linköping municipality)** | Sparsely populated area, farms, detached houses | 800 households | Eon | 25–30% |
| **Kiruna (central town + surrounding areas)** | Smaller town, mining community | 3,000 households | Vattenfall Eldistribution | 20–25% |

**Steps to form a cooperative grid**:

1.  **Interest group**: At least 10% of the customers in the area sign a letter of intent. The municipality or an existing association (e.g., community association) takes the initiative.
2.  **Feasibility study**: The cooperative receives a grant (SEK 200,000 from the Swedish Energy Agency) to investigate the grid's technical condition, cost structure, and potential savings.
3.  **Negotiation with the grid owner**: The cooperative negotiates a lease agreement specifying the lease fee (based on the grid owner's reported capital costs), division of responsibilities (who fixes faults, who invests in new cables), and transfer of metering data.
4.  **Formation of the cooperative**: An economic association is registered with the Swedish Companies Registration Office (Bolagsverket). The members subscribe for shares (typically SEK 1,000–5,000 per household). The shares are used as working capital.
5.  **Takeover**: The cooperative signs agreements with contractors for operation and maintenance (same as before or new ones). Meter reading and billing are set up.
6.  **Operations**: The cooperative bills the members for the grid fee (lower than before). The surplus is refunded or invested in smarter grids (batteries, load management).

**Risk management**:

  - The cooperative takes out insurance for unforeseen breakdowns (e.g., a transformer fire).
  - A reserve fund is built up (10% of the annual fee) for future reinvestments.
  - The lease agreement contains a "repossession clause": if the cooperative mismanages (e.g., goes bankrupt), the grid owner can repossess the grid at no extra cost.

**Evaluation**:

  - Reduction of the grid fee in percentage compared to before (target: \>20%).
  - Members' satisfaction (survey).
  - Operating cost per customer (compared to the grid owner's historical costs).
  - Number of operational disruptions (should be unchanged or lower).
  - Proportion of members actively participating in annual meetings and board elections (target: \>15%).

| Item | Amount (per pilot) | Total 3 pilots |
|------|--------------------|------------------|
| Feasibility study grant (SEK 200,000) | SEK 0.2 million | SEK 0.6 million |
| Legal assistance (agreements, registration) | SEK 100,000 | SEK 0.3 million |
| Technical consultant (grid mapping) | SEK 150,000 | SEK 0.45 million |
| Start-up capital (member contributions, covers working capital) | SEK 1–2 million (loans/contributions) | SEK 3–6 million (not a state cost) |
| Administrative support (first year) | SEK 300,000 | SEK 0.9 million |
| Evaluation | SEK 200,000 | SEK 0.6 million |
| **Total state cost** | **SEK 0.95 million per pilot** | **SEK 2.85 million** |

**Financing**: The state's cost (SEK 2.85 million) is very low in relation to the long-term savings for electricity customers. EU funds (LIFE, Horizon) could possibly also be used. The start-up capital from members is an investment that yields a return in the form of lower grid fees (as a rule, the contribution is repaid within 2–4 years). The municipality can act as a guarantor or co-financier.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Electricity Act (1997:857)** – a new section enabling user cooperatives to take over the management of the local grid: *"The electricity grid concession holder (the grid owner) is obliged, at the request of a cooperative consisting of at least 500 customers within a defined grid area, to grant the right of use to the grids within the area for a reasonable fee corresponding to the grid owner's capital and operating costs."*
2.  **Amendment to the Economic Associations Act (1987:667)** – clarification that cooperatives for grid management are permitted and that profit distribution (refunds) to members is tax-free if it is based on surplus from the fees.
3.  **The Swedish Energy Markets Inspectorate's regulations** – Ei must approve the lease fees (so that the grid owner does not charge unreasonably high fees). A model for cost-based lease fees is developed.
4.  **The Swedish Tax Agency** – clarify that the lower grid fee is not a taxable benefit for the members, as it is a refund of surplus from a cooperative association.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "The grid owners (Eon, Vattenfall, Ellevio) will oppose this by all means – it is their most profitable business."

**Answer**: Yes, they will lobby against it. But the government can force them via the Electricity Act. The grid owner still gets their capital cost covered via the lease fee – they only lose the extra profit that exceeds a reasonable return. It is difficult for the grid owner to argue that this profit is socially beneficial. In Germany and the USA, similar models have been introduced despite strong opposition from energy companies.

### "The cooperatives lack competence – who will fix the grid when there is a fault?"

**Answer**: The cooperative hires the same contractors as the grid owner uses (or others). The difference is that the cooperative itself decides who gets the assignment and at what price. Many smaller electrical companies are interested in taking over maintenance from the large companies. The competence exists in the market.

### "It will be expensive for the cooperative to build up administration and billing."

**Answer**: Billing can be outsourced to a smaller billing company or managed via a digital platform (costing a few kronor per customer and month). Administration can be minimal – a part-time coordinator is sufficient for 500–1,000 customers. The large grid companies have heavy administration with management teams, marketing, and lawyers – the cooperatives avoid this.

### "What happens when the grid needs to be reinforced? The cooperative might not have capital."

**Answer**: The lease agreement regulates the investment responsibility. Normally, the grid owner owns the cables and transformers – the cooperative is responsible for operation and maintenance, but major reinvestments (e.g., a new transformer station) are made by the grid owner (and financed via a raised lease fee). The cooperative can also choose to finance certain investments itself if it is cheaper. There is flexibility.

### "Are there Swedish examples?"

**Answer**: No full-scale ones, but closely related ones. **Municipal electricity grids** (e.g., Malmö, Gothenburg) are a form of local ownership, but they are still monopolies with political governance, not member-controlled cooperatives. **The Swedish Association of Energy Societies** (Sveriges Energiföreningars Riksförbund, SER) has pushed the issue of local grid cooperatives for many years. **Fiber cooperatives** are widespread in Sweden (thousands of villages have built their own fiber networks). The technology and organizational model are proven – why shouldn't electricity grids be able to work in the same way?

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Fiber cooperatives** (e.g., Bygdenät, local fiber associations) – over 1,000 villages and city districts in Sweden have their own fiber networks. The members own the network and pay low fees. The same model for electricity grids.
  - **Municipal electricity grids** (e.g., Malmö Energi, Göteborg Energi) – often have lower fees than private grids. Shows that non-profit-maximizing ownership lowers costs.
  - **Cooperative wind farms** (Denmark, Germany) – member-owned and governed. Experience with member democracy and financing.
  - **Norway's "lokale nettselskap" (local grid companies)** – Norway has many small grid companies, often owned by municipalities or local energy agencies. Some of them have cooperative elements.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are the chair of a housing cooperative or a community association**: Talk to the neighbors. Check which electricity grid company you have and what you pay. Request a quote from the grid owner to lease the grid. Even if they say no – you have started a process.

**If you are a municipal politician**: Submit a proposal that the municipality should investigate the possibility of forming a municipal grid cooperative (in collaboration with municipal housing companies). Use fiber cooperatives as a model.

**If you are a Member of Parliament**: Submit a motion for a legislative amendment that forces grid owners to grant the use of grids to cooperatives (see the proposal above). Highlight the thousands of Swedish fiber cooperatives as proof that the model works.
