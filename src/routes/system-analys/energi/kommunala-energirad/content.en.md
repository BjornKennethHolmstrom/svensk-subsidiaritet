# Municipal energy councils

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Each municipality establishes an energy council consisting of residents, business representatives, local grid owners, environmental organizations, and municipal officials. The council has formal veto power (or decisive influence) over new large-scale energy projects—wind farms, large-scale solar parks, new power lines, hydrogen plants—planned within the municipality. The decision is based on local acceptance, impact on industries (reindeer husbandry, tourism, agriculture), and the environment. The goal: break the deadlock where central decisions are made without local anchoring, which leads to appeals, delays, and conflicts. By giving local actors real influence, predictability for project developers increases—they know that if the energy council says yes, there will be no appeals.

## The mechanism

Today, large-scale energy projects are reviewed in several instances: the municipality (detailed development plan or building permit), the county administrative board (environmental permit), the land and environment court (appeals), and sometimes the government (permit under the Electricity Act). The municipality has a so-called "municipal veto"—it can say no to wind power within the municipal borders. But the veto is rarely used, because it is politically sensitive and can be overturned in some cases. The result: projects receive permits despite local protests, which leads to long appeal processes (5–10 years) and ultimately often that the project is abandoned.

**The measure**: A **municipal energy council** is a broadly composed group that:

  - Is appointed by the municipal council for a term of office (4 years). The council has 11–15 members:
      - 3 residents (elected through citizen dialogue or lottery)
      - 2 representatives for local business (chamber of commerce, business association)
      - 1 representative for affected Sami villages (in northern Sweden) or farmers (in the south)
      - 1 representative for local environmental organizations (The Swedish Society for Nature Conservation, local initiatives)
      - 2 representatives for the local grid owner (Eon, Vattenfall, municipal electricity grids)
      - 2 politicians (from different parties, not the chair)
      - 2 officials (the municipality's head of environment and building permits, planning architect)
      - Chairperson (external, e.g., a retired judge or university teacher)
  - The council has **veto power** (or a strong recommending role, with a requirement that the municipal council follows the decision unless 2/3 vote against). The veto applies to:
      - Wind farms with more than 3 turbines or a total height over 150 m.
      - Solar parks of more than 10 hectares.
      - New power lines of voltage class 130 kV or higher (which require a permit under the Electricity Act).
      - Hydrogen or battery parks above a certain size.
  - The process:
    1.  The project developer applies for an advance ruling from the energy council *before* formal permits are sought.
    2.  The council requests documentation, holds public consultations, and gathers feedback.
    3.  The council makes a decision within 6 months: "approved", "approved with conditions" (e.g., compensation measures to the local community), or "rejected".
    4.  If the council rejects, the project developer cannot proceed (or must appeal to the land and environment court, but with a very high burden of proof). If the council approves, the project is in principle "pre-secured"—local appeals are not accepted in later instances (exception: national interests that cannot be resolved locally).

**Why this solves today's problems**:

| Problem today | The energy council's solution |
|--------------|----------------------|
| Municipal vetoes are rarely used (political cowardice) | The energy council is broad and apolitical – easier to say no |
| Projects are appealed by many different groups (villagers, environmental organizations, reindeer owners) | All groups are represented in the council – their views are handled early |
| The permitting process takes 5–10 years | The council's decision is made within 6 months – the rest of the process is faster |
| The project developer does not know if the project is accepted until after million-kronor investments | Advance ruling before large costs – reduces risk |
| Local protests lead to media attention and political crises | The council's decision is anchored – few surprises |

## Pilot design – 5 municipalities first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑3)**: Five municipalities in different parts of the country establish energy councils with veto power (according to the model above). The government grants an exemption from the current Planning and Building Act during the pilot period.

**Proposed pilot municipalities**:

| Municipality | Why? | Expected conflict |
|--------|---------|--------------------|
| **Piteå** | Planned large-scale wind power (Markbygden) – many protests from reindeer owners and villagers | High |
| **Öland** | Many plans for wind power and solar parks – the tourism industry is worried about the impact | High |
| **Vara** (Västra Götaland) | Agricultural area, resistance to solar parks on arable land | Medium |
| **Åre** | Wind power on the mountain – clash with tourism and reindeer husbandry | High |
| **Halmstad** | Offshore wind power (sea outside) – conflict between environmental interests and fishing | Medium |

**Organizational model**:

  - The council is appointed by the municipal council after an open application process (for residents, business, etc.).
  - The council has a secretary (municipal employee or external consultant) who handles administration and investigations.
  - The project developer pays a fee to have their case reviewed (SEK 50,000–200,000, depending on project size). The fee covers the council's costs.
  - The council's decisions are public and must be justified in writing. The decision can be appealed to the land and environment court, but only by the project developer (if they consider the decision violates the law)—not by a third party (villagers, organizations). This is to reduce the number of appeals.

**What happens if the council says no?**:

  - The project cannot be implemented within the municipality, unless the government or the land and environment court overturns the veto due to "compelling national interests" (e.g., national energy preparedness). This exception is used very restrictively.
  - The project developer can propose compensation measures (e.g., build a new school, finance local road maintenance, provide annual compensation to the Sami village) and ask for a new review.

**Evaluation**:

  - Number of projects approved, approved with conditions, and rejected.
  - Average time from application to decision (target: `<`6 months).
  - Proportion of the council's decisions that are appealed (target: `<`5%).
  - Proportion of projects that are actually built after an approved decision (compared to today's 30–50%).
  - Surveys to project developers, municipal politicians, and residents regarding satisfaction with the process.

The remaining SEK 400,000/year per municipality can be financed by the municipality itself (as an investment in predictability and reduced conflicts) or by the state (via the Swedish Energy Agency). SEK 14.5 million over 3 years is a modest sum in context.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Planning and Building Act (2010:900)** – introduce a new section: *"The municipality may establish an energy council with veto power over large-scale energy projects according to the appendix. The council's decision shall be binding for the municipality and for subsequent permit review, unless the government decides otherwise for special reasons."*
2.  **Amendment to the Environmental Code (1998:808)** – for projects approved by the energy council, the right of appeal by third parties shall be limited. This is to avoid local protests being moved from the municipality to the court. (Corresponding rules already exist for certain infrastructure projects.)
3.  **Ordinance on the composition of energy councils** – the government establishes minimum requirements for representation (residents, business, Sami villages, environmental organizations). The municipalities can add more members.
4.  **Appeal process** – the project developer's opportunity to appeal a rejection decision is reviewed by the land and environment court, which can only overturn the veto if it is "manifestly unreasonable" or violates "compelling national interests".

## Expected resistance and answers

### "The municipalities will shut everything down – no projects will happen."

**Answer**: On the contrary. Today, municipalities often say yes to projects that the local population opposes, which leads to appeals. With the energy council, the municipality gets a clear signal in advance—and can deny projects that would be stopped by appeals anyway. The developer avoids spending millions on projects that won't happen anyway. In Germany, where similar models exist (Bürgerenergiegenossenschaften), the proportion of built projects has increased.

### "Environmental organizations and Sami villages will get too much power – they can block everything."

**Answer**: They have one vote each, not a majority. The council consists of many different interests. If all projects are blocked, the pressure from businesses and politicians will change the composition in the next term. The system is self-regulating.

### "This is undemocratic – a small group will decide important energy issues."

**Answer**: The council complements, rather than replaces, the democratic process. The municipal council appoints the energy council and can alter its mandate. All decisions are public. Residents still have the opportunity to exert influence via municipal politics. The difference is that a broad group (with local expertise) makes decisions on technically complex issues—just like a building committee or environmental committee.

### "Project developers will go straight to the government to bypass the veto."

**Answer**: The exception possibility exists ("compelling national interests") but should be used very restrictively—typically if the project is of crucial importance to the stability of the electricity system or national security. For most projects, the energy council's decision applies. Developers know this and adapt: they present better projects, listen to local opinions, and offer compensation.

### "Are there any Swedish examples?"

**Answer**: Not exactly, but closely related. **Municipal wind power companies** (e.g., Skellefteå Kraft) are a form of local influence. **Consultation under the Environmental Code** is mandatory but rarely has decisive power. **Village communities and Sami villages' veto power** exist in some cases (Reindeer Husbandry Act), but not collectively. The pilot would be unique in Sweden but resembles models in **Germany** (Bürgerinitiativen with veto power in certain federal states) and **Scotland** (community benefit funds with veto). We can learn from them.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Municipal wind power companies** – Skellefteå, Piteå, Övertorneå. They often have local reference groups, but no formal veto power.
  - **Consultation under the Environmental Code (Chapter 6)** – statutory, but not binding.
  - **Sami villages' right of consultation** – they have the right to express their views, but no veto.
  - **Denmark's "Lokalplaner for vindmøller"** – municipalities in Denmark have detailed development plans that identify suitable areas. Residents can exert influence in the planning process. Our model goes one step further.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal politician**: Propose in the municipal council to establish an energy council (in pilot form). Use the text above as a basis. Offer to participate in drafting the statutes.

**If you are an engaged citizen (villager, Sami, environmentalist, entrepreneur)**: Gather like-minded people. Write a letter to the municipal executive board: "We want an energy council with real influence. Let us be part of designing it."

**If you are a project developer (wind power, solar power, batteries)**: Support the idea—you win on predictability. Lobby industry organizations (Swedish Wind Energy Association, Swedenergy) to push for municipal energy councils. It is better than today's lottery.
