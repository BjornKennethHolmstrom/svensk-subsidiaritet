# Distributed Processing Infrastructure

[← Back to main article](..)

**Summary**: Denmark has 30 mobile slaughterhouses—Sweden has zero, due to regulation rather than technology. This section presents three regional pilots (Jämtland, Skåne, Västra Götaland), the Danish model, concrete cost estimates, and the regulatory changes required.

## The Measure

Mobile and cooperative processing units (slaughterhouses, mills, fermentation plants, dairies) that serve multiple small producers. This reduces the capital requirements that currently force consolidation and allows small-scale producers to process their raw materials locally instead of sending them to centralized facilities—or being forced to sell unprocessed goods at low prices.

**Economies of scope**: Instead of seeking large-scale economies of scale (which drive centralization), distributed processing achieves economies of scope—shared infrastructure serving varied production at an appropriate scale. A mobile slaughter unit can serve 20 farms within a 50-kilometer radius. A cooperative mill can grind grain from 30 growers. A shared fermentation plant can produce fermented vegetables, kombucha, or lactic acid products for several farms simultaneously.

## Swedish Barriers – Why Doesn't It Already Exist?

### Regulatory Barriers

- **Slaughterhouse regulations**: Swedish slaughterhouses must meet the same hygiene requirements regardless of scale. Mobile slaughterhouses are practically impossible because the rules require separate rooms for clean and unclean operations, which is difficult in a truck. Denmark has exemptions for small-scale mobile units—Sweden does not.
- **Dairy regulations**: Farm dairies are subject to the same control programs as Arla. The cost of certification and sampling makes small-scale cheese and yogurt production unprofitable.
- **Mills**: The EU hygiene regulation (EC 852/2004) is interpreted differently in different member states. In Sweden, a separate mill building with floor drains and stainless steel is often required—in Denmark, simpler solutions are permitted for direct sales.

### Economic Barriers

- Investment subsidies from the EU go almost exclusively to large-scale equipment. A small mill for SEK 500,000 rarely receives support; a large mill for SEK 50 million does.
- Banks are unaccustomed to financing cooperative infrastructure. An individual farmer cannot get a loan for a mobile slaughter unit—but a cooperative with ten members has better prospects, although banks lack models for evaluating such projects.

## Swedish Role Models and Exceptions

- **The Dalsland slaughterhouse** – a smaller, cooperative slaughterhouse that has survived through hard work, but struggles with regulations written for large-scale operations.
- **Klövermjölk farm dairy** (Skåne) – an exception that proves it is possible, but the founders testify to a three-year permit process and costs of over half a million SEK before the first liter was sold.
- **The REKO rings' own mill initiatives** – in several REKO groups, farmers have joined forces to buy a shared mill, but are forced to use it illegally or transport grain to a large mill in another region.

## The Danish Model

Since 2015, Denmark has permitted mobile slaughterhouses for up to 500 animals per year per unit. The rules do not require separate rooms, just thorough cleaning between animals and documented hygiene routines. The result: over 30 mobile units in operation, serving thousands of small farms. Swedish adoption requires political will and regulatory changes—not technological development.

## Proposed Pilot – Three Regions, Three Types

### Region 1 (Jämtland) – Mobile Slaughter Unit

- **Technology**: Slaughter container with fold-down walls, cleaned between each animal. The same model as in Denmark.
- **Regulatory change**: The County Administrative Board grants a time-limited exemption. Evaluation after 2 years.
- **Organization**: Economic association with 15 farms sharing the investment (approx. SEK 1.5 million). State investment subsidy of 50%.

### Region 2 (Skåne) – Cooperative Mill and Bakery

- **Technology**: Stone mill (SEK 300,000) and dough mixer (SEK 100,000) in a converted container. Rented out to growers and bakers.
- **Regulatory change**: The Swedish Food Agency issues a guiding decision that container solutions are approved for direct sales under 10 tons/year.
- **Organization**: Cooperative society where each member buys shares (SEK 5,000 each). Members get priority access to the mill and a discount.

### Region 3 (Västra Götaland) – Fermentation Hub

- **Technology**: Fermentation vessels (100–500 liters), cold room, packaging line. Shared by 10 vegetable growers.
- **Regulatory change**: No new law is required, but the County Administrative Board recognizes the hub as a shared food premises (instead of each grower needing their own permit).
- **Organization**: Cooperative lease agreement—growers pay per hour used and per liter of fermented product.

## Regulatory Changes Required

1. **Introduce a new category in the Food Act**: "Small-scale mobile unit" with simplified requirements (separate rooms are not required, just cleaning protocols).
2. **Change the EU subsidy criteria** so that 30% of investment support is earmarked for mobile and cooperative units under SEK 2 million.
3. **Create a national "green lane"** for permits—one administrator per county with specific expertise in small-scale, distributed processing, with a maximum processing time of 6 months.

## Financing and Economics

- **Investment**: A mobile slaughter unit costs SEK 1.2–1.8 million. With a 50% investment subsidy, this becomes SEK 600,000–900,000 shared among 15 farms = SEK 40,000–60,000 per farm.
- **Operations**: A butcher at 50% employment (SEK 300,000/year) plus cleaning, administration, and transport. At 500 slaughters per year, the cost is about SEK 150 per slaughter + margin. This is competitive with large-scale slaughterhouses that charge SEK 100–200 for slaughter but require long transports.
- **Socio-economics**: Each mobile unit creates 1–2 local jobs, reduces animal transport (lower emissions, less stress), and enables small farms to survive. An evaluation of Danish mobile slaughterhouses showed a socio-economic return of 2.5 times the investment.

## Objections and Answers

- *"This is too expensive—we cannot subsidize every little unit."* – The subsidy is a one-time investment, not ongoing. Danish experience shows that mobile units become self-sustaining after 2–3 years.
- *"Hygiene cannot be guaranteed in mobile units."* – The Danish model has worked for almost a decade with fewer remarks than stationary small slaughterhouses. The problem is Swedish regulatory conservatism, not technology.
- *"Why not just build more small stationary slaughterhouses?"* – Because mobility is crucial for serving sparsely populated areas. A stationary unit requires animals to be transported. The most expensive and stressful thing for the animals is precisely the transport.
