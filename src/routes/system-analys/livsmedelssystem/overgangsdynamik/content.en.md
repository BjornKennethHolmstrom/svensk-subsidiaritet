# Transition Dynamics: How Systems Actually Change

[← Back to main article](..)

The three phases in the implementation plan are a logical sequence, but the transition will not follow a straight line. Complex adaptive systems (like food systems) change through **disruptions, experiments, and portfolio strategies**—not through central planning. Therefore, we need a conscious design for the dynamics of the transition.

## 1. Safe-to-Fail Experiments – Not Fail-Safe

**The principle**: In a complex system, you cannot predict what will work. Therefore, you should conduct many small experiments that **can fail without the system collapsing**. This is the opposite of large-scale pilots that are "too big to fail."

**Swedish application**:
- **Ten pilot municipalities** in phase 1 are already safe-to-fail—if three fail, the other seven continue. It is important to evaluate openly and share failures, not just successes.
- **Mobile slaughterhouses** in three regions—different technical solutions (slaughter truck, container solution, cooperative facility). Let the regions choose differently, evaluate after two years, and scale up what works.
- **Digital platforms**—allow multiple parallel codebases (different open-source projects) that compete and learn from each other. No "national platform" that locks everyone in.

**What it requires**: Political and administrative tolerance for failure. Municipalities must dare to report "this didn't work" without being punished. A possible model: **trial operations under Section 24 of the Local Government Act**, which already allows deviations from laws in limited projects.

## 2. Disruptions as Accelerators

**The principle**: Crises (pandemics, war, energy price shocks, extreme weather) are not just threats—they are **windows for system change**. A resilient system utilizes disruptions to accelerate the transition, rather than just restoring the old.

**Swedish application**:
- **Link emergency preparedness to the transition**. When the next crisis hits (presumably energy prices or crop failures in Europe), use the political momentum to temporarily allow exemptions from rules that hinder distribution (e.g., slaughter rules for mobile units, direct sales without certification requirements in an emergency).
- **Preventative scenario training** – County Administrative Boards and the Swedish Civil Contingencies Agency (MSB) invite municipalities, farmers, and cooperatives to "stress tests": "What happens if Ukraine shuts down its grain exports? What happens if the price of diesel quadruples?". Based on the answers, the most vulnerable rules are identified—and then abolished in advance, not in a panic.
- **"Emergency boxes"** – use an actual disruption to test them. If a storm stops deliveries for a week, activate the emergency boxes in two municipalities and evaluate.

**Example from Sweden**: During COVID-19, the government temporarily allowed farm-level slaughter and direct sales of meat. It worked. The rules were reintroduced after the crisis. Next time, they should be made permanent.

## 3. Portfolio Strategy – Not "One Size Fits All"

**The principle**: Uncertainty requires diversification not only in production but also in strategy. A portfolio of different initiatives (technology, organization, policy) survives better than a single effort.

**Swedish application**:
- **Three parallel tracks**:
  - *Municipal track*: Procurement, land, planning.
  - *Cooperative track*: Farmers joining together in new forms (economic associations, co-owned infrastructure).
  - *Civil society track*: REKO groups, farming networks, consumer cooperatives.
- These tracks do not need to be synchronized. They can develop at different paces and cross-pollinate each other. If one track fails (e.g., municipalities backtrack after an election), the others can continue.

**Metrics for the portfolio**: Not just "share of local food," but also the **number of active initiatives, number of municipalities with preparedness plans, number of new farmers under 40, number of cooperative units**. A portfolio is measured on diversity, not on the maximization of a single variable.

## 4. Panarchy and Scale-Crossing

**The principle**: In complex systems, change occurs when an innovation "jumps" from one scale level to another—from a farm to a municipality, from a municipality to a region, from a region to the nation.

**Swedish application**:
- **From farm to village**: A farmer succeeds with direct sales. Neighbors see it and join in. Soon the village has its own food node.
- **From village to municipality**: The municipality sees the success and adapts its procurement. Now more farmers get access to public meals.
- **From municipality to region**: Several municipalities in the same region coordinate their procurement requirements, creating scale without centralization.
- **From region to nation**: The government sees that the regions have reduced their dependence on imports and changes subsidy rules after the fact (not before).

**Practical measure**: **An annual "scale-crossing workshop"** where actors from different levels (farmer, municipal director, county governor, agency) meet and identify bottlenecks for scale-crossing. The workshop produces a concrete list of rules that should be changed—and sends it to the responsible ministry.

## 5. Negative Capacities: Not Acting Too Early

**The principle**: In complex systems, it is sometimes best to **wait and observe** instead of acting. Introducing a national solution too early can lock in a subpar design.

**Swedish application**:
- **Delay national regulations** of platforms, direct sales, and slaughterhouses—let the phase 1 experiments run for three years before drawing conclusions.
- **Avoid "one size fits all" standards** for organic certification, traceability, and the working environment. Allow regional and cooperative variants.
- **Use sunset clauses**—all temporary exemptions are written with an automatic expiration date, but can be extended if they work.

## Integration With the Phases

- **Phase 1** (1–3 years): Focus on safe-to-fail experiments and building a portfolio. Allow failures. Gather data.
- **Between phase 1 and phase 2** (years 2–4): Evaluate the portfolio. Identify which experiments succeeded. Use scale-crossing workshops to move successes upwards.
- **Phase 2** (3–7 years): Scale up what has worked, but retain diversity. Introduce permanent rule changes based on evidence from phase 1.
- **Phase 3** (7–15 years): The system now has multiple parallel structures (central, regional, local). The transition dynamics continue—new disruptions will come, new experiments will be needed.

## Objections and Responses

- *"This sounds like unplanned experimentalism—we need decisive action, not academic fluff."* – On the contrary. Planned experimentalism is the only strategy that works in complex systems. Pretending that we can predict everything is naive and leads to an overreliance on central planning.
- *"Who coordinates all these experiments?"* – No one. Coordination arises through shared infrastructure (digital platforms, shared evaluation criteria), not through a chain of command. That is the point.
- *"How do we avoid failures damaging trust in the entire transition?"* – By **communicating failures as learning**. Every pilot municipality commits to publicly reporting both successes and failures in a standardized template. The role of politics is to protect the experiments from being prematurely condemned.
