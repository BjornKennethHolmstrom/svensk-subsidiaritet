# Distributed healthcare infrastructure

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Transform today's primary care centers from disease-focused clinics to health-focused centers that integrate multiple disciplines and are located in community spaces such as libraries, schools, community centers, or shopping malls. Instead of concentrating resources in large hospital complexes, primary care is moved closer to people's everyday lives – physically, culturally, and relationally. The teams consist of health guides, nurses, dietitians, therapists, and doctors working collaboratively rather than hierarchically. The goal: to make health a natural part of life, not a separate institutional domain, and to reduce geographical and social inequality in access to healthcare.

## The mechanism

Today's primary care centers are often located in separate buildings with signs signaling "here are the sick". They are designed for efficient patient management: reception, waiting room, examination room, out. This is functional for acute visits but undermines preventive and health-promoting work. Many hesitate to go to the primary care center "unnecessarily", which means that early signs of ill health are ignored until they become acute.

**The measure**: A **distributed healthcare infrastructure** means that:

  - Primary care resources are spread out into several smaller units in the local community, preferably in premises already used for other purposes (co-utilization).
  - Each unit is a **health center** rather than a primary care center. The focus is on promoting health, not just treating disease.
  - The teams are interprofessional and work team-based, where health guides and nurses handle the majority of contacts and doctors are reserved for complex medical assessments.
  - The premises are inviting, low-threshold, and designed to encourage spontaneous visits, conversations, and community.

**Comparison: Traditional primary care center vs. Distributed health center**:

| Aspect | Traditional primary care center | Distributed health center |
|--------|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| Location | Own building, often on the outskirts of a residential area | Integrated into a library, community center, school, shopping mall |
| Opening hours | 8–17, closed for lunch | Adapted to the rhythm of the local community (evenings, weekends) |
| First contact | Telephone queue, appointment booking | Drop-in, health desk, digital booking |
| Staff | Doctors, nurses, assistant nurses | Doctors, nurses, health guides, dietitian, counselor, pharmacist |
| Focus | Diagnosis and treatment | Health promotion, prevention, self-care, community |
| Atmosphere | Clinical, sterile | Homey, inviting, informal |

**Examples of design**:

  - **The Library Health**: Adjacent to the library, there are a couple of rooms for conversations and simple examinations, as well as an open "health desk" where health guides answer questions about diet, exercise, sleep, and provide guidance.
  - **School Health 2.0**: The school's student health service is supplemented with a family center where parents can meet a child health center (BVC) nurse, parenting counselor, and health guide in the same building as the child's school.
  - **Shopping Mall Health**: A space in a shopping mall or at a commuter train station where you can take your blood pressure, get health advice, pick up prescribed prevention activities, and meet a nurse without an appointment.

## Pilot design – 3 local health centers in different environments

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑4)**: Three existing primary care centers are transformed into distributed health centers with new premises, working methods, and team composition.

| Pilot | Type of area | New placement | Team (in addition to doctor/nurse) | Special focus |
|-------|--------------|--------------|-------------------------|----------------|
| **Hammarby Sjöstad, Stockholm** | Newly built urban area, mixed population | Ground floor in a residential building, neighbor to library and park | 2 health guides, dietitian, physiotherapist, counselor | Families with children, stress-related illness |
| **Västerås, Bäckby** | Million Programme area, socio-economic challenges | Converted school premises, co-located with youth center and open preschool | 3 health guides (multilingual), family counselor, pharmacist | Integration, chronic disease, mental illness |
| **Åre municipality** | Sparsely populated area and tourism, long distances | Existing community center in Duved, supplemented with digital care rooms | 1 health guide, district nurse, access to doctor via video | Elderly care, distance-bridging technology |

**Working methods in practice**:

  - **The Health Desk**: Staffed by health guides who receive spontaneous visits, answer questions, and help with digital healthcare contacts. No appointment is required.
  - **Team round**: Every morning, the team gathers to go through the day's patients and distribute tasks based on competence. The doctor focuses on complex cases, the others handle advice, follow-up, and prevention.
  - **Joint activities**: The health center regularly organizes open activities such as walks, cooking classes, parent meetings, and lectures – often in collaboration with study associations and clubs.
  - **Digital integration**: All patients have access to a digital platform where they can see their health plan, book appointments, chat with their health guide, and track their health data.

**Evaluation**:

  - Total number of visits and distribution between planned doctor visits, drop-in, digital contacts, and group activities.
  - Patient satisfaction (survey) – perceived availability, treatment, continuity.
  - Change in the number of emergency visits and hospital admissions for listed patients compared to control primary care centers.
  - The staff's work environment and sick leave.
  - Cost per listed patient compared to a traditional primary care center.

**Financing**:

  - **Region Stockholm / Västmanland / Jämtland Härjedalen**: The pilots are financed within the framework of the transition to "Nära vård" (Close care). The regions can apply for state grants from the National Board of Health and Welfare for "Good and close care".
  - **Municipal co-financing**: Municipalities have an interest in reducing social services costs and can contribute premises and staff (e.g., family counselors).
  - **Research funds**: Forte, Vinnova, and the EU's structural funds can finance the evaluation.

**Note**: In the long term, the model is expected to be cost-neutral or cheaper per patient thanks to reduced emergency consumption, fewer hospital admissions, and more efficient use of resources.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **The Health and Medical Services Act (HSL)**: Clarify that primary care's mission includes health-promoting and preventive work that can be carried out in collaboration with municipalities and civil society. Open up for primary care to be conducted in premises co-utilized with other operations.
2.  **The Act on Medical Care Remuneration and Choice of Care**: Adapt the remuneration models so that they reward team-based work, prevention, and continuity, not just the number of doctor's visits.
3.  **The Planning and Building Act (PBL)**: Introduce requirements that new residential areas and community properties must contain spaces for health centers or similar health-promoting functions.
4.  **The Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act**: Enable smooth information sharing between region, municipality, and civil society when the patient consents. A uniform consent model is developed.
5.  **The Work Environment Act**: Adapt the regulations to make it easier for staff to work in interprofessional teams and in premises outside traditional healthcare environments.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This will be more expensive – more premises and more staff."

**Answer**: The investment in premises and more health guides is offset by reduced demand for more expensive specialist care and emergency visits. Furthermore, the co-utilization of premises enables lower rental costs per square meter.

### "Patients want to see a doctor, not a health guide."

**Answer**: Those who need a doctor will get to see a doctor – and they will have shorter waiting times because the doctor is relieved of other duties. However, many patients are just as satisfied (or more satisfied) with getting quick help from a knowledgeable health guide for their non-medical needs.

### "It will be messy with different professions – who is responsible?"

**Answer**: Clear role descriptions and teamwork reduce the risk of misunderstandings. The doctor retains medical responsibility for the patient's care plan but does not perform all tasks themselves. The model is similar to the one already used in advanced home healthcare (ASIH) and provides high patient safety.

### "Rural areas cannot get the same offerings as the city."

**Answer**: Distributed infrastructure is not about copying the city's model, but adapting it to local conditions. In sparsely populated areas, a health center can be a combination of digital services, mobile teams, and a physical meeting place in the community center. The important thing is that the resources are close to the people, not that the building is large.

### "Is there evidence that this works?"

**Answer**: Yes, from several sources. **The Nuka model** in Alaska has reduced emergency visits by over 50% through team-based primary care. **Buurtzorg** in the Netherlands has revolutionized home healthcare with self-managing teams. **Jönköping County** has long worked with the Esther model for cohesive care. What is missing is a systematic Swedish application of these principles in primary care.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **The "Nära vård" transition**: The national reform to move care closer to the patient. Distributed healthcare infrastructure is a concrete way to realize the vision.
  - **Family centers**: Co-location of maternity care, child healthcare, open preschool, and social services. Works well but only reaches families with children. The concept can be broadened to all ages.
  - **Digital primary care centers (e.g., Kry, Min Doktor)**: Shows that many healthcare contacts can take place digitally and that physical presence is not always necessary.
  - **Hospital libraries and health squares**: Already established low-threshold operations that can form the core of a distributed health center.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a regional politician**: Visit an existing family center or a health square. Ask the operations manager what is required to scale up the model to all ages. Bring the insights to the next budget preparation.

**If you are a primary care center manager**: Start by freeing up time for your nurses to work more preventively. Identify a local partner (library, study association) and arrange a joint theme day on health.

**If you are a municipal official**: Make an inventory of the municipality's premises that could be co-utilized for health-promoting activities. Invite the region to a collaboration meeting regarding shared premises.

**If you are an architect or urban planner**: Start sketching what an integrated health center could look like in your specific municipality. Use social sustainability requirements in detailed development plans to reserve spaces for health.
