# Teacher cooperatives

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Teachers organize as cooperative enterprises rather than as municipal employees. The cooperative signs an agreement directly with a group of guardians (at least 50 families) or with a municipality to run a school or part of a school's operations. The teachers own and govern the cooperative together (one member, one vote), share the surplus, and have full control over pedagogy, schedules, and work distribution. The goal: to increase teachers' professional autonomy, improve compensation (by cutting administrative middlemen), and reduce teacher flight. The model is based on existing legislation on independent schools (Education Act, Chapter 24) but is adapted to favor teacher-owned cooperatives over commercial companies.

## The mechanism

Today, the vast majority of teachers are employed by municipalities (or in some cases by independent school corporations). Salaries, working hours, schedules, and pedagogical methods are governed by central agreements and political decisions. Teachers have little influence over their own working situation—even though they are highly educated professionals. The result: 40% quit within five years.

**The measure**: Introduce **teacher cooperatives** as a new legal and organizational form to run school operations.

  - **How a cooperative is formed**: At least 5 certified teachers join forces and form an economic association (cooperative) according to the Economic Associations Act (1987:667).
  - **How it is financed**: The cooperative signs an agreement with a municipality (or directly with guardians) to provide education for a certain group of students. The school voucher (approx. SEK 70,000–120,000 per student and year) goes directly to the cooperative.
  - **How salaries are set**: The teachers are co-owners and employees of the cooperative. They jointly decide on salary levels (often higher than municipal teachers, since middlemen and administrative overhead are cut). The surplus is reinvested in the operations or distributed as a bonus.
  - **How quality is ensured**: The cooperative must follow the same curriculum, the same Education Act, and the same inspection as all other schools. Guardians can opt out of the cooperative if they are dissatisfied (the school choice remains).
  - **Difference from existing independent schools**: Today, most independent schools are run by profit-driven companies (often owned by private equity firms). Teacher cooperatives are **non-profit-maximizing**—any surplus goes to the teachers or back into the operations, not to external owners.

**Advantages compared to municipal employment**:

| Aspect | Municipal school | Teacher cooperative |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Decisions on pedagogy | Politicians, officials, principal | The teachers themselves in a democratic process |
| Workload | Often high, directed from above | Flexible, can be adapted to the assignment |
| Administrative burden | Documentation for the municipality | Documentation for own operations (can be streamlined) |
| Salary | Fixed in central agreement | The cooperative decides (can be higher) |
| Ownership | None | Joint ownership, share of surplus |

**Practical example**: A group of 8 teachers in a medium-sized city forms a cooperative. They sign an agreement with the municipality to take over the responsibility for an existing school (grades F–6) with 120 students. The school voucher is SEK 90,000 per student → total revenue SEK 10.8 million. The teachers budget for salaries (8 × SEK 550,000 = SEK 4.4 million), premises (SEK 2.5 million), materials (SEK 1 million), and administration (SEK 1 million). SEK 1.9 million remains, which can be used for continuing education, extra resources, or a rebate to the members. Each teacher thus receives the equivalent of a salary of about SEK 700,000—a substantial increase from a municipal salary (approx. SEK 450,000). At the same time, the administrative burden is reduced since there is no need to report to a municipal administration.

## Pilot design – 5 cooperatives first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑3)**: Five teacher cooperatives are started in different parts of the country (different school types and sizes). The state or a foundation offers **start-up loans** (SEK 1–2 million per cooperative) and advice during the first year.

**Proposed pilots**:

| Location | Type | Number of teachers | Number of students | Special profile |
|-------|-----|--------------|--------------|------------------|
| Gothenburg | Compulsory school F–6 | 12 | 180 | Reggio Emilia pedagogy |
| Umeå | Lower secondary 7–9 | 8 | 120 | Nature and technology profile |
| Landskrona | Compulsory school F–9 | 15 | 240 | Bilingualism (Swedish/Arabic) |
| Falun | Upper secondary special school | 6 | 40 | Practical vocational training |
| Stockholm | Preschool | 5 | 60 | Outdoor pedagogy (in the forest) |

**Steps to form a cooperative in the pilot**:

1.  **Expression of interest**: At least 5 certified teachers send an expression of interest to a national **support organization for teacher cooperatives** (e.g., the Cooperative Institute or a new foundation).
2.  **Business plan**: The group develops a simple business plan: budget, premises, catchment area, pedagogical profile.
3.  **Financing**: The support organization grants a start-up loan (interest-free for the first 2 years) of SEK 1–2 million to cover initial costs (consulting, legal, marketing).
4.  **Agreement with municipality**: The group negotiates with one or more municipalities to receive school vouchers for a certain group of students. The municipality cannot refuse if the cooperative meets the legal requirements (school choice applies).
5.  **Registration**: The cooperative is registered with the Swedish Companies Registration Office (Bolagsverket) and the Swedish Schools Inspectorate (as an independent school). Special exemption rule for the pilot: lower capital requirements (SEK 100,000 instead of SEK 200,000).
6.  **Start**: Operations kick off after the summer holidays.

**Evaluation**:

  - Teachers' job satisfaction (rating scale) and retention in the profession after 2 years.
  - Teachers' salaries compared to municipal teachers in the same region.
  - Students' knowledge results (national tests, grades) compared to control schools (matched).
  - Parents' satisfaction (SALAR's user survey).
  - The cooperative's finances: does it survive, does it run at a profit/loss?

## Budget and financing

| Item | Amount (for 5 cooperatives) |
|------|---------------------------|
| Start-up loans (interest-free, SEK 1.5 million on average × 5) | SEK 7.5 million (repaid) |
| Support organization (advice, training, legal) | SEK 2 million/year |
| Evaluation (research) | SEK 1.5 million/year |
| **Total first year** | **SEK 11 million** (7.5 loans + 3.5 costs) |

**Financing**: The start-up loans can come from the state budget (Ministry of Education) or from a fund (e.g., the Swedish Agency for Economic and Regional Growth, the European Regional Development Fund). The costs for support and evaluation are low (SEK 3.5 million/year) compared to the potential savings if teacher flight decreases (a single teacher who stays an extra year saves society SEK 1–2 million in recruitment and induction costs).

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Education Act (2010:800) Chapter 24 on independent schools** – add a section: *"Independent schools run as economic associations where the majority of members are certified teachers active in the school shall be given priority when assessing establishment. The government may grant exemptions from the equity requirement for such cooperatives during a pilot period of at least three years."*

2.  **Amendment to the Economic Associations Act (1987:667)** – no obstacle, but clarify that a teacher's employment and membership can be combined.

3.  **Ordinance on start-up loans** – the government authorizes an agency (e.g., the Swedish National Agency for Education) to grant interest-free start-up loans to teacher cooperatives in the pilot.

4.  **Agreement between cooperative and municipality** – SALAR (Swedish Association of Local Authorities and Regions) develops a standard template for agreements regulating school vouchers, premises, student responsibility, and quality follow-up.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "The municipalities will say no – they don't want to lose control over the schools."

**Answer**: School choice already gives parents the right to choose an independent school today. The municipality cannot refuse an independent school that meets the legal requirements. On the contrary: teacher cooperatives can be attractive to the municipality because they are often more cost-effective (less administration) and have lower staff turnover. The municipality can also enter into an agreement where the cooperative runs a municipal school on a contract basis—in this way, the municipality retains ownership but outsources the operations.

### "The teachers' unions will object – cooperatives threaten collective agreements."

**Answer**: The cooperative's teachers can still be affiliated with the union (the Swedish Teachers' Union or the National Union of Teachers in Sweden). The cooperative can sign a collective agreement with the union. Experiences from other industries (e.g., healthcare cooperatives, consulting cooperatives) show that unions can adapt. Furthermore: the unions are concerned about teacher flight. Cooperatives are a possible answer to that crisis.

### "It will become an elite operation – only good teachers at attractive schools can start cooperatives."

**Answer**: The risk exists. Therefore, the pilot is small and carefully evaluated. Support can also be directed to cooperatives in vulnerable areas (higher school vouchers, extra start-up capital). The goal is not to replace all municipal schools with cooperatives, but to create a diverse system where cooperatives are one option among several.

### "Teachers don't want to be entrepreneurs – they want to teach."

**Answer**: In a cooperative, teachers can delegate administration and finance to a hired manager (who does not have to be a teacher). The cooperative can also merge with other cooperatives to share common functions (payroll processing, cleaning, IT). Many teachers are attracted by the opportunity to influence their working situation and share in the surplus. For those who do not want to—municipal employment is still an option.

### "Are there Swedish examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, several small-scale ones. **Montessori schools** are often run as non-profit associations, sometimes with teachers on the board. **Parent cooperatives** (preschools) exist in many municipalities, where parents hire teachers. But purely teacher-owned cooperatives in compulsory schools are rare. The closest is **"Vittra"**, which was started as a teacher cooperative in the 1990s but later became a limited company. The pilot can revive the idea. Internationally, there are **"teacher cooperatives"** in the USA (e.g., Milwaukee), Spain (Escola Sadako), and Finland. Sweden is lagging behind.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Parent cooperatives (preschools)** – have existed since the 1970s, regulated in the Education Act. The model for collaboration between members exists.
  - **Employee-owned companies (e.g., consulting cooperatives)** – within IT, architecture, and journalism, there are cooperatives where the employees own and govern. The experiences are good (higher productivity, lower sick leave).
  - **Health and social care cooperatives** – e.g., Vardaga started as a cooperative. Elderly care cooperatives exist in several municipalities. The Swedish National Agency for Education can emulate this structure.
  - **Norway's "lærersamarbeid"** – Norway has several schools run as collaborative projects between teachers, without commercial owners. Sweden can visit and learn.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a teacher in a group of at least 5 colleagues**: Talk about the idea. Write a joint expression of interest to your municipality and to SALAR. Ask for a meeting to discuss the possibility of starting a cooperative. Use the text above as a basis.

**If you are a municipal politician or official**: Develop a policy for how the municipality can support teacher cooperatives (e.g., by providing premises at cost price, offering advice, being a constructive contracting party). Raise the issue in the municipal executive board.

**If you are a union representative**: Investigate how your union relates to cooperatives. Are there obstacles in central agreements? Can a collective agreement be signed with a cooperative? Be a bridge-builder, not a roadblock.
