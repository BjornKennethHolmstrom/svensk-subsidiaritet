# Personal welfare budgets

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Long-term recipients of multiple benefits (income support, housing allowance, maintenance support, introduction benefit, etc.) receive a "welfare card" that combines all parts into a single monthly amount. The card is loaded with a fixed sum based on the household's size and needs (but is higher than the sum of the benefits because administration and control cost money). The recipient decides how the money is distributed among rent, food, electricity, childcare, clothes, education, and savings. Unused funds can be saved or used for approved investments (e.g., driver's license, vocational training). The goal: dignity, flexibility, and incentives to work and save, instead of today's poverty trap where every extra krona earned leads to withdrawn benefits.

## The mechanism

Today, many long-term benefit recipients live in a **poverty trap** where the system actively discourages work and savings. A single parent receiving income support, housing allowance, maintenance support, and possibly introduction benefit faces marginal taxes of over 100%: every hundred kronor earned from part-time work leads to benefits being reduced by more than a hundred kronor. The result: no rational person works. The system locks people into exclusion.

**The measure**: Introduce **personal welfare budgets** (also called "welfare cards" or "combined benefits") for long-term recipients who meet the following criteria:

  - Have been dependent on income support or other means-tested benefits for at least 12 months (which signals that they are stuck in the system)
  - Lack addiction or other problems that require special case management (in such cases, traditional models are retained)
  - Can manage their own budget (assessed by a social worker, but the presumption is that most adults can)

**What the card contains**:

The card is loaded every month with an amount based on:

  - Household size (adults, children)
  - Housing cost (rent or fee, up to a ceiling)
  - Special needs (e.g., dietary supplements in case of illness, travel to labor market programs)

The amount is **higher** than the sum of the benefits the person would otherwise receive—because administration and control are dramatically reduced (no monthly means testing, no arbitrary deductions). Experiences from similar systems show that one can be 10–20% above the benefit level and still save money for society.

**What the card can be used for** (standard):

  - Rent and electricity (can be paid automatically)
  - Food and household items
  - Childcare, leisure activities for children
  - Clothes, shoes
  - Public transport, fuel (reasonable level)
  - Medicines, dental care (outside the high-cost threshold)
  - Savings (up to SEK 1,000/month, must not exceed a 6-month buffer)

**What the card can be used for** (after approval):

  - Education (vocational courses, driver's license, computer license)
  - Investments in work tools (computer, work clothes)
  - Moving costs (to take a job in another location)

**What the card may NOT be used for** (blocked):

  - Alcohol, tobacco, gambling
  - Luxury goods (designer clothes, expensive restaurant visits—but normal restaurants are fine)
  - Travel abroad (exceptions: funerals, acute family needs)

**Incentives for work**:

  - Every krona earned from work **does not affect** the card's amount during the first 6 months. Thereafter, the card is reduced by 50 öre per earned krona (marginal tax of 50% instead of today's 100%+).
  - Unused funds on the card (what is not used during the month) can be saved and used for approved investments, or taken out as a cash bonus after 12 months (max SEK 5,000). This creates an incentive to be frugal—the opposite of today's "use-it-or-lose-it".

## Pilot design – 5,000 people first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: 5,000 long-term benefit recipients in 10 municipalities get access to a personal welfare budget. The selection is made by lottery among qualified individuals (to enable a randomized control group).

**Selection criteria**:

  - Income support for at least 12 months (continuous or with short breaks)
  - Age 25–60 years
  - No ongoing addiction investigation or LVU placement (Care of Young Persons Act) (such cases retain traditional case management)
  - Can read and write Swedish (or with the support of an interpreter)

**Organizational model**:

  - Each pilot municipality appoints a "budget coach" (1 per 200 participants) who informs, helps with technical issues, and supports budget planning if necessary.
  - The card is administered via an existing platform (e.g., bank card linked to a digital account, similar to a payroll card). Several banks already offer "social cards"—these can be procured.
  - Payment is made monthly automatically, based on housing cost and household size (updated every 6 months).
  - Spot checks: 2% of transactions are reviewed automatically (blocked categories). In case of suspected fraud—the card is frozen and the case reverts to traditional case management.

**Evaluation** (compared to a randomized control group):

  - Proportion moving from benefits to work (primary efficacy endpoint)
  - Time to work (months)
  - Self-reported quality of life, health, perceived dignity
  - Administrative costs per person (expected to decrease by 50–70%)
  - Incidence of fraud and abuse (expected to be low)

## Budget and financing

| Item | Annual cost (5,000 participants) | |
|------|----------------------------------| |
| Higher benefit amount (15% above normal level, average SEK 3,000/month × 5,000 × 12) | SEK 180 million |
| Budget coaches (5,000/200 = 25 coaches at SEK 400,000) | SEK 10 million |
| Card administration (bank fee, platform, SEK 200/person/year) | SEK 1 million |
| Spot checks (administrator time) | SEK 0.5 million |
| Evaluation (research) | SEK 2 million |
| **Total first year** | **SEK 193.5 million** |
| **Annual operation after scale-up** | **SEK 190 million** (excl. evaluation) |

**Financing**: Today's costs for income support, housing allowance, maintenance support, and introduction benefit for 5,000 people are approximately SEK 250–300 million (depending on composition). Even with a 15% increase, the pilot is below current costs—because administration and control decrease drastically. If only 10% of the participants go to work (and thus leave the benefit systems), the pilot is self-financing.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Social Services Act (2001:453)** – add a new section: *"The social welfare committee may, after individual assessment, grant income support in the form of a personal welfare budget paid monthly on a special card. The budget shall be higher than the combined benefit level by at least 10 percent, corresponding to administrative savings."*
2.  **Amendment to the Housing Allowance Ordinance (2001:933)** – for people with welfare cards, the housing allowance becomes a component of the card (no separate application). This requires an exemption from the main rule.
3.  **Amendment to the Income Tax Act** – to avoid the card's payouts being taxed as income. They remain tax-free benefits, but since they are not means-tested monthly, this must be clarified.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is a waste – people will buy booze and gambling with the money."

**Answer**: That is why certain categories are blocked on the card. But even if a few abuse the system, it is better that 95% gain dignity and flexibility than that 100% are suffocated by bureaucracy. Experiences from similar systems (e.g., Canada's "cash transfers" for the homeless) show that the vast majority use the money wisely—often more wisely than social workers do.

### "Municipalities lose control – they need to be able to assess the need every month."

**Answer**: The needs of a long-term benefit-dependent person do not change dramatically from month to month. Annual reassessment is sufficient. The time and energy freed up when administrators are relieved of monthly forms can be spent on helping people into work instead of checking their receipts.

### "This is a hidden cut – people get less than today."

**Answer**: On the contrary—we propose an increase of 10–20% compared to current benefit levels. But even if the sum is the same, the recipient gets the opportunity to prioritize for themselves. Today, a single mother is forced to spend exactly SEK 4,700 on food (social services' standard amount) even if she would rather spend SEK 4,000 on food and SEK 700 on a training course for her child. The flexibility is worth at least as much as the money.

### "People who cannot manage their finances will end up on the street."

**Answer**: That is why the pilot is voluntary and aimed at people who are deemed able to manage a budget. Those who obviously cannot (addiction, severe mental illness, dementia) retain traditional models with a trustee. And budget coaches are available for those who need support—a resource that today's system lacks.

### "Are there any international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes. Canada's "Ontario Works" tested personal budget cards with positive results (reduced administration, higher satisfaction). The UK's "Universal Credit" is in practice a personal budget, although the implementation has been problematic. Finland's basic income experiment showed that people do not abuse freedom. Sweden is lagging behind—the pilot can provide Swedish evidence.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Income support with a "reasonable standard of living"** – standard amounts already exist today. The welfare card is an extension of the same logic, but with individual distribution.
  - **Payroll cards** – many employers already use prepaid cards for staff. The same technology can be used for benefits.
  - **Activity support (The Swedish Public Employment Service)** – paid monthly without requiring receipt accounting. It works. Why can't income support do the same?
  - **Denmark's "kontanthjælp" (cash benefit) with a job plan** – Denmark has simpler models, but still detailed regulation. Sweden's pilot can be even simpler.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a social worker or unit manager**: Identify 10 long-term benefit recipients who you assess would be able to handle a personal budget. Ask them: "If you got to decide for yourself over a pot of money every month, would you be able to handle it?" Test informally with an agreement (the municipality pays a fixed amount, the person accounts for it afterwards). Evaluate after three months.

**If you are a municipal politician**: Submit a proposal for a pilot with personal welfare budgets for 100 people. Use the text above as a basis. Ask for a randomized control group to be able to evaluate the effect on work and quality of life.

**If you are a systems developer or fintech entrepreneur**: Build a prototype for a welfare card with category blocking, monthly reloading, and a simple user app. Offer municipalities to test it for free for six months.
