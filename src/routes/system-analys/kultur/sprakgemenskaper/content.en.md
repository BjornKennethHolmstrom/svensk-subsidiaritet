# Language community councils

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: 1% of the national cultural budget (approx. SEK 120 million) is reserved for cultural production in languages other than Swedish. The funds are distributed by language community councils—one for Arabic, one for Kurdish, one for Somali, one for Finnish, etc.—based on the number of speakers in each municipality. A Kurdish poet in Botkyrka can finally receive funding to write in Kurdish, without the requirement of a Swedish translation. The goal: to stop economically forced language death.

## The mechanism

Today, almost all cultural support (the Swedish Arts Council, municipalities, regions) requires that the application and the finished production are in Swedish—or at least translated into Swedish. This forces cultural creators with a different mother tongue to either:

  - Translate their work (expensive, time-consuming, and much is lost in translation)
  - Forgo support and produce anyway (made invisible)
  - Abandon their language entirely and write/sing in Swedish (assimilation)

**The measure**: Create **language community councils**—self-governing bodies for every major language group (defined as at least 5,000 speakers in Sweden). The councils receive:

  - An annual pot based on the number of speakers in Sweden (adjusted for municipal distribution)
  - Full decision-making authority over how the money is used (grants for poets, musicians, theaters, magazines, podcasters)
  - The right to assess applications in their own language, without the requirement for Swedish translation
  - Simple administration via a digital platform

**What the money can be used for**:

  - Production of literature, music, performing arts, film, podcasts in the language
  - Language preservation projects (documentation of dialects, oral traditions)
  - Translation *from* Swedish into the language (the reverse of today)
  - Guest performances and tours within the language community (e.g., an Assyrian singer from Södertälje visiting Norrköping)

## Which languages get councils?

**Criterion**: At least 5,000 speakers in Sweden (according to Statistics Sweden's language statistics, or self-reporting). This results in councils for:

| Language | Estimated number of speakers | Annual pot (1% of cultural budget per capita) |
|-------|------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| Arabic | 400,000 | SEK 48 million |
| Kurdish (Sorani/Kurmanji) | 100,000 | SEK 12 million |
| Somali | 70,000 | SEK 8.4 million |
| Finnish (incl. Meänkieli) | 200,000 | SEK 24 million |
| Bosnian/Croatian/Serbian | 80,000 | SEK 9.6 million |
| Persian (Dari/Farsi) | 50,000 | SEK 6 million |
| Tigrinya | 25,000 | SEK 3 million |
| Greek | 15,000 | SEK 1.8 million |
| Turkish | 10,000 | SEK 1.2 million |
| **Total** | **approx. 950,000** | **SEK 114 million** |

(The remaining approx. SEK 6 million of the 1% pot goes to administration and smaller language groups under 5,000, who share a common pot.)

## Pilot design – four languages first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: Start councils for the four largest language groups: Arabic, Kurdish, Somali, Finnish/Meänkieli. Each council receives a smaller pot the first year (20% of the full level) to build the organization.

**The Arabic council** (example):

  - Headquarters: Malmö (where many Arabic speakers live, but the council operates nationally)
  - Board: 7 members elected by a census among Arabic speakers (simple election via SMS, one person one vote)
  - First year's budget: SEK 9.6 million (20% of SEK 48 million)
  - Task: Announce grants for poetry, short films, music production in Arabic. Build a website where applications are made in Arabic, assessed by Arabic speakers.

**The Somali council**:

  - Headquarters: Rinkeby (Stockholm) or Borlänge
  - Board: 5 members
  - First year's budget: SEK 1.7 million

**Evaluation after 2 years**:

  - Number of applications per language (target: at least 100 the first year)
  - Proportion of funds going to previously marginalized cultural creators (without previous support from the Arts Council)
  - Linguistic vitality—measured through a survey of speakers on whether they feel the language is used in cultural life more than before

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Swedish Arts Council's instruction (SFS 2015:162)**—add a new assignment: *"The Arts Council shall annually allocate 1% of its appropriations distributed to language community councils in accordance with the appendix. The language community councils decide independently on the distribution of grants within their language group."*
2.  **New ordinance on language community councils**—defines how the councils are appointed (election among speakers, minimum transparency requirements, requirement for annual reports in Swedish and in the language).
3.  **Exemption from the Discrimination Act**—since the grants only go to a specific language group, it could be perceived as discrimination against Swedish speakers. Add an exemption: *"Differential treatment on the basis of language is permitted when the purpose is to promote cultural diversity and preserve minority languages."*

## Budget and financing

| Item | Annual cost |
|------|--------------|
| 1% of the Arts Council's budget (SEK 12 billion × 0.01) | SEK 120 million |
| Of which to language communities (according to the table above) | SEK 114 million |
| Of which to administration (the Arts Council's processing of the councils) | SEK 6 million |

**Financing**: Reallocation within the Arts Council. SEK 120 million is 1%—a minor reprioritization from large institutions. Alternatively, financing can be sourced from the Swedish Agency for Support to Faith Communities (which already has a similar model for religious groups).

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is segregation—we are supposed to integrate, not be divided."

**Answer**: Integration is mutual. Today, minority language speakers are forced to adapt to Swedish to receive cultural support—that is assimilation, not integration. A true integration ideal allows different groups to express themselves in their own languages, and then meet in translation and exchange. Language community councils create visibility, not invisibility.

### "Why should Swedish taxpayers finance Arabic poetry?"

**Answer**: Because Arabic speakers are Swedish taxpayers. They pay taxes just like everyone else. Today, they get almost none of their tax money back in the form of cultural support that reflects their language and traditions. It is an injustice. And besides: Arabic poetry produced in Sweden *is* Swedish culture—not foreign.

### "The councils will be controlled by strongmen or religious forces."

**Answer**: That is why we require democratic elections within each language community (one person, one vote) and the publication of minutes and decisions. The risk is always there, but it also exists in the current system (e.g., that a small group of Stockholm curators controls the entire cultural budget). Transparency and the ability to dismiss council members are the remedy.

### "Small language groups receive disproportionately little."

**Answer**: The per capita distribution is proportional. A group of 5,000 speakers receives 5,000/950,000 of the pot—approx. 0.5% = SEK 600,000/year. This is enough for a part-time position and a few small projects. It is fair. If you want to protect very small languages (e.g., Romani Chib, with a few thousand speakers), you can earmark an extra pot for "urgent language preservation."

### "Won't the Arts Council sabotage this through bureaucracy?"

**Answer**: That is why we propose a separate ordinance that gives the councils independence. The Arts Council's role is limited to paying out money and verifying that the councils follow democratic rules—not assessing the quality of the applications.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **The Sweden-Finnish Association (Sverigefinska folkförbundet)**—already has a Finnish-language cultural committee, but without guaranteed funding.
  - **The Swedish Agency for Support to Faith Communities**—distributes money to religious groups based on the number of members. The same model, but for languages.
  - **The Sami Parliament's Culture Council**—distributes funds to Sami culture, including language preservation. Has been functioning since 1993. Why shouldn't Arabic speakers have the same right?
  - **The Language Council of Norway**—provides support for Norwegian sign language literature. Finland has the "Society of Swedish Literature" for Swedish-speaking Finns. Sweden is lagging behind.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a Member of Parliament**: Submit a motion to introduce language community councils with 1% of the cultural budget. Use the Sami Parliament as a model.

**If you are active in a language community**: Collect signatures from speakers in your municipality. Send them to the Committee on Cultural Affairs. Show that the demand exists.

**If you are a culture secretary in a municipality**: Start on a small scale—allocate SEK 50,000 for "translation support" for local poets in minority languages. Test the model locally.
