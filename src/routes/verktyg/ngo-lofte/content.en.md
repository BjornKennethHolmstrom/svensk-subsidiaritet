# A Subsidiarity Pledge for International NGOs
### From Service Delivery to Local Empowerment

**Preamble**
International NGOs play a vital role in humanitarian response and development. Yet the traditional model—funding controlled in Geneva, strategies designed in London, programs implemented by local staff with limited decision-making power—perpetuates a power imbalance that undermines the very resilience we seek to build. True empowerment requires shifting authority, resources, and accountability to the communities and local organizations we work with.

These commitments build on—and go beyond—the sector's existing localization promises (e.g., the Grand Bargain's still-unmet 25% direct-funding target). We recognize that local and national actors already deliver aid more cost-efficiently in many contexts; the barrier is power and money, not capacity.

Subsidiarity is not a management technique—it is a recognition that legitimacy, knowledge, and agency originate locally.

---

### The Six Commitments

**1. 50% Local Leadership by 2030**
We commit to a public, time-bound goal of ensuring that at least **50% of our country directors and senior field leadership positions are held by nationals of the country they serve**, with a preference for candidates originating from or deeply embedded in the affected communities. We will publish an annual progress report detailing current percentages, hiring pipelines, and barriers encountered—including transparent reporting on socio-economic and geographic background, with **explicit safeguards against capital-city elite capture** (e.g., weighted scoring for candidates from affected provinces or marginalized groups).

**2. Direct Funding to Local Organizations**
We commit that by 2030, at least **25% of our total program funding** (excluding unavoidable head-office costs and rapid-onset emergency surge funding) will be directly transferred to local and national civil society organizations, without international intermediaries controlling implementation. Furthermore, we commit that an increasing proportion of this direct transfer will be provided as **unrestricted or core funding**, recognizing that local organizations require institutional autonomy to build long-term resilience, not just project-by-project survival. We will publish annual data on the proportion of funds reaching local partners.

**3. Participatory Budgeting with Scaling Commitments**
We commit to piloting **Participatory Budgeting** in at least one major refugee camp, displacement site, or community program in year one, **prioritizing stable or protracted settings where security and logistics allow community deliberation**, and to scaling the practice so that within five years, **at least 25% of applicable programs** include direct community voting on budget allocation. We will define a "significant portion" of funds subject to community vote as no less than **10% of the project budget**, with a pathway to increase this over time. Results—including voter turnout and community satisfaction—will be published in the local language.

**4. Exit Strategy as a Success Metric**
We will evaluate our programs not by their longevity, but by **how quickly and completely they transition to local ownership**. Every major project will have a publicly stated exit timeline, tied to **co-developed indicators of local capacity and autonomy**, not arbitrary calendar dates. We will report annually on progress toward that transition. Permanence is not the goal; empowerment is.

**5. Transparency Downward with Feedback Accountability**
We commit to publishing all project budgets, major decisions, and evaluation findings in the **local language(s) and accessible formats** (audio, visual, plain language) for the communities we work with. Furthermore, we will fund an **independent, locally-led Ombudsman's office** in our largest field operations, with the authority to receive confidential complaints and report directly to our global board. We commit to **publicly responding to all substantiated findings within a defined timeframe**, including actions taken or justification for inaction, and will publish the Ombudsman's annual summary on our global website.

**6. Data Sovereignty and Epistemic Subsidiarity**
We commit to the principle of data subsidiarity: **data collected from communities belongs to those communities**. We will ensure that local actors have full access to, and ownership over, the aggregate data and research generated about their own populations. Furthermore, we commit to **co-designing evaluation metrics with affected communities**. Success will not merely be measured by donor-imposed logical frameworks, but by indicators of well-being, resilience, and impact as defined by the local context.

---

### What This Pledge Is Not

- **Not anti-international cooperation.** Global solidarity and resource transfer remain essential. This pledge is about *how* that cooperation happens.
- **Not abandonment of responsibility.** Shifting power does not mean walking away. It means changing the nature of the relationship.
- **Not symbolic localization.** These commitments are measurable and time-bound, designed to resist performative compliance.

---

### A Call to Action

**For NGO Staff & Supporters:** Ask your organization's leadership to publicly endorse this pledge. Start a conversation about which commitment your office could pilot first.

**For Donors & Foundations:** Ask the NGOs you fund whether they adhere to these six principles. Consider making funding contingent on meaningful progress toward at least one of them.

**For Concerned Citizens:** Share this pledge. Ask the NGO that knocked on your door how many of their country directors are local nationals—and how much of their budget flows directly to local organizations. Your question matters more than your one-time donation.

---

*This pledge is offered as a public good. It was developed with inspiration from the principles of subsidiarity (decisions made as close to the affected community as possible) and distributed governance. It may be freely adapted, translated, and used by any organization or individual seeking to shift power in the international aid sector.*
