# Restorative justice

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: For minor offenses – theft under SEK 5,000, vandalism, property damage, simple assault without serious injuries – a court can refer to mediation between victim and offender instead of prosecution. A trained mediator (community mediator or specially appointed person) leads a conversation where the offender gets to meet the victim, understand the consequences, apologize, and repair the damage (through work, repayment, or community service). If the agreement is fulfilled, the prosecution is dropped. The goal: reduce the burden on the justice system, increase the victim's redress, and reduce recidivism – at a fraction of the cost of prison or fines.

## The mechanism

Today, even minor offenses are handled by a heavy, expensive, and slow legal process: police investigation, prosecutor, court, possibly prison or fines. The victim is often a bystander – the process is about punishing the offender, not about repairing the damage. The offender rarely learns why the action was wrong, and recidivism rates are high. At the same time, a single year in prison costs over SEK 1 million.

**The measure**: Introduce **restorative justice** as an alternative to the traditional legal process for minor offenses, where:

  - The crime is admitted or proven (the perpetrator confesses or the evidence is clear)
  - The victim consents to mediation (voluntary)
  - The crime is of such a nature that prison is not required in the penalty scale (maximum penalty under 2 years, or obvious that the sanction would be fines/probation)
  - A trained mediator (independent, often from civil society or a special mediation unit) leads the process

**The process in steps**:

1.  The prosecutor assesses that the crime is suitable for restorative justice. Prosecution is not initiated immediately; instead, the case is sent to a **mediation center** (municipal or regional).
2.  The mediator contacts the victim and the offender separately, explains the process, and asks if they want to participate (both must say yes).
3.  The mediation takes place in a safe room – often in premises that are not a court (library, citizen center, community hall). The mediator leads the conversation, but the victim and the offender talk directly to each other.
4.  If the parties reach an agreement – e.g., the offender apologizes, pays damages, performs community service (cleaning a park, helping at a nursing home), or takes a conflict management course – it is written down and signed.
5.  The agreement is followed up by the mediator after 1 month, 3 months, and 6 months. If it is fulfilled, the prosecutor drops the charges.
6.  If the agreement is not fulfilled, or if any party changes their mind, the case goes back to the prosecutor for normal processing.

**Why restorative justice works better**:

| Aspect | Traditional justice | Restorative justice |
|--------|----------------------|--------------------|
| The victim's role | Bystander, often re-traumatized | Active party, receives redress |
| The offender's learning | Punished, but rarely understands why | Meets the victim, understands consequences |
| Recidivism | High (30–50% for youths) | Lower (studies show a 10–20% reduction) |
| Cost | High (trial, possibly prison) | Low (mediation costs SEK 5,000–15,000) |
| Time | Months to years | A few weeks |
| Societal effect | Division (victim isolated, offender labeled) | Healing (relationships are repaired) |

**Example**: A 17-year-old boy has smashed a window at a school in frustration. Traditionally: police report, interrogation, prosecutor, court – fines (parents pay) or youth care. Restoratively: the boy meets the principal and the janitor who had to clean up. He apologizes, promises to repaint the fence as an extra effort, and writes a reflection on why he got angry. The principal accepts. Prosecution is dropped. The boy learns something. The school gets a painted fence.

## Pilot design – 3 regions first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1‑3)**: Three regions (e.g., Stockholm, Västra Götaland, Skåne) introduce restorative justice for selected crime types. The pilot area covers 5–10 municipalities per region.

**Included crime types** (exclusive list, can be expanded):

  - Petty theft (max SEK 5,000)
  - Vandalism (max SEK 10,000 in damage, no fire or dangerous object)
  - Minor assault (bruises, scratches – no hospital care)
  - Unlawful threat (lower degree, no weapon)
  - Arbitrary conduct (e.g., taking someone's bicycle without permission)
  - Burglary in a storage room or garage (lower degree)

**Excluded crimes** (always go to court):

  - Violence with a weapon (knife, gun)
  - Aggravated assault (broken bones, hospital stay)
  - Sexual offenses
  - Crimes against children under 15 years old
  - Repeated serious criminality

**Organizational model**:

  - The Swedish Prosecution Authority trains administrators to identify suitable cases.
  - The mediation centers are established by the regions or municipalities in collaboration with civil society (e.g., Victim Support Sweden, Save the Children, local mediation centers).
  - The mediators are recruited from experienced community mediators, social workers, psychologists, or lawyers – and undergo an 80-hour special training in restorative justice.
  - An **independent review board** (with representatives from the police, prosecutors, victim organizations) quality-assures the mediation process and handles complaints.

**Evaluation** (compare with a control group taking the traditional route):

  - Proportion of mediations leading to an agreement (target: \>80%)
  - Proportion of agreements fulfilled (target: \>90%)
  - Recidivism in crime within 1 year and 2 years (expected to be 10–20% lower than the control group)
  - The victim's experience of justice and redress (survey scale 1–10)
  - Cost per case (expected SEK 5,000–15,000 vs. SEK 30,000–100,000 for a court process)
  - Processing time (expected 4–6 weeks vs. 4–6 months)

## Budget and financing

| Item | Annual cost (3 regions, an estimated 2,000 cases per year) |
|------|------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Mediation centers (3 × 1 center at SEK 2 million) | SEK 6 million |
| Mediators (fee per case SEK 5,000 × 2,000) | SEK 10 million |
| Training of mediators (80 hrs × 50 mediators × SEK 500/hr, one-time) | SEK 2 million |
| Independent review board | SEK 1 million |
| Evaluation (research) | SEK 2 million |
| **Total first year** | **SEK 21 million** |
| **Annual operation after scale-up** | **SEK 17 million** (excl. training) |

**Financing**: Compared to the court system's budget (approx. SEK 7 billion/year) or the prison and probation service's costs (SEK 11 billion/year), SEK 21 million is a very small investment. Every case that goes to mediation instead of court saves SEK 25,000–90,000. At 2,000 cases per year, the saving is SEK 50–180 million – the pilot pays for itself several times over.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Code of Judicial Procedure (1942:740)** – add a new section (RB 20:8a): *"If the crime is of a minor nature and the perpetrator has confessed, the prosecutor may refer the case to mediation according to the Act on Restorative Justice. Prosecution may only be initiated after the mediation has proven unsuccessful."*

2.  **New Act on Restorative Justice** – defines the mediation process, the mediator's role, confidentiality, the victim's rights, the effect of agreements, and supervision.

3.  **Exceptions to the Secrecy Act** – the mediator must be able to promise confidentiality. Exceptions from the Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act (OSL) for information that emerges during mediation (similar to lawyers and psychologists). Mandatory reporting upon suspicion of serious crimes remains.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is letting criminals go free – they should be punished, not talk."

**Answer**: The victim gets a chance for redress, the offender is forced to take responsibility in a way that prison never achieves. Studies show that recidivism decreases. Merely punishing without repairing is both expensive and inefficient. Restorative justice is not a "talk" – it is a demanding process with clear requirements for restitution.

### "The victim may feel pressured to participate."

**Answer**: The mediation is completely voluntary for the victim. The victim is carefully informed of their rights and can cancel at any time. If the victim says no, the case goes to court as usual. However, many victims appreciate the opportunity to tell the perpetrator how the crime affected them.

### "The offender might pretend to be remorseful to avoid punishment."

**Answer**: The mediator is trained to detect fake remorse. If the agreement is not fulfilled (e.g., the offender ignores the community service), the case returns to the prosecutor. And an agreement requires concrete actions (work, payment) – not just words.

### "Prosecutors won't use this option – they are conservative."

**Answer**: That is why training and incentives are required. The pilot regions commit to using restorative justice in at least 5% of suitable cases. Over time, it becomes a natural part of the toolbox. Experiences from Norway and the UK show that prosecutors quickly become positive when they see the results.

### "Are there international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, many. New Zealand has used restorative justice for youths since 1989 ("Family Group Conferences") with very good results (recidivism reduced by 20–30%). The UK, Canada, and several US states have similar models. Norway introduced a law on restorative justice for youths in 2020. Sweden is lagging behind – the pilot can provide Swedish evidence.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Victim-Offender Mediation (LVM)** – has existed since 2008 for young offenders, but is rarely used and is not linked to dropping charges. Our model provides clear legal status.
  - **Young offenders – special processing** – social services can already conduct youth investigations, but the process is heavy. Restorative justice is smoother.
  - **Mediation Center in Uppsala** – has existed for several years, but works mostly with family conflicts. The scale model exists.
  - **Norwegian "konfliktråd" (conflict councils)** – Norway has local conflict councils handling restorative justice for youths. Sweden can copy the organization.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a prosecutor or judge**: Test informally – in a simple case (e.g., vandalism), suggest to the parties that they meet a mediator before the main hearing. If they reach an agreement, ask for a lesser penalty. Document the result.

**If you are a municipal politician or official**: Contact the Swedish Prosecution Authority and offer to set up a mediation center in pilot form. Use the text above as a basis. Offer premises and mediators.

**If you are a lawyer or social worker**: Train to become a mediator in restorative justice (courses are available via, e.g., Victim Support Sweden, Save the Children, or international organizations). Become a resource in your region.
