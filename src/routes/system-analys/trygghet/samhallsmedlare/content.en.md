# Community mediators

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: 100 respected local figures – coaches, religious leaders, grandmothers, former gang members – are recruited and trained in conflict mediation (200 hours). They receive a monthly salary of SEK 25,000 and work to intervene in disputes and recruitment attempts long before the police need to be involved. The goal: to create a credible, non-governmental actor that has legitimacy in the community and can resolve conflicts where police authority is insufficient.

## The mechanism

Today, many conflicts in vulnerable areas escalate into shootings and deadly violence – not because the police do not want to intervene, but because police presence often lacks legitimacy. Residents do not trust the police, youth refuse to talk, and conflicts between groups are instead handled by gang leaders or informal "judges". The result: a parallel justice system that reinforces criminal structures.

**The measure**: Introduce **community mediators** – trusted individuals from the community who:

  - Have high legitimacy in the area (know people, understand cultural codes, speak the same language)
  - Are trained in conflict mediation, de-escalation, child and youth rights, and when the police must be involved
  - Receive a salary (SEK 25,000/month) to work part-time or full-time actively going out into the area, listening to youth, identifying brewing conflicts, and mediating between parties
  - Have a clear role: **intervene before violence** – not to replace the police in serious crimes, but to catch conflicts at a level where they can still be resolved through conversation and negotiation

**What the mediator does** (examples of tasks):

  - Meets youth at youth centers, cafes, mosques, football pitches – listens to concerns, pulls strings.
  - In a conflict between two families or youth groups: invites both parties to a mediation session (in a safe location, often a community hall or library).
  - Negotiates a solution – an apology, repayment of stolen goods, one party changing schools – that is accepted by both.
  - Documents the agreement (in writing or verbally) and follows up after a week, a month.
  - If the conflict is too serious (weapons involved, threat of deadly violence) – involves the police's mediators or security coordinators.

**Why the mediator works better than the police**:

  - **Legitimacy**: A grandmother in the area has an authority that the police lack. A former gang member can talk to youth in a way a uniformed police officer never can.
  - **Early intervention**: The mediator catches conflicts while they are still small – before anyone has bought weapons or recruited friends.
  - **Long-term perspective**: The mediator remains in the area after the conflict, building relationships and trust over time. Police officers are often rotated away.
  - **Cost**: A mediator costs SEK 300,000/year (salary + payroll taxes). A police officer costs SEK 1.2–1.5 million/year. The mediator can handle many more conflicts per hour because they do not have the same bureaucracy.

## Pilot design – 100 mediators first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: 100 community mediators are hired in 20–30 municipalities with the greatest needs (high crime, vulnerable areas, perceived insecurity). The mediators are placed in areas where they already have roots and trust.

**Selection process**:

  - The municipality or an independent foundation (e.g., Save the Children, Sweden's City Missions) is responsible for recruitment.
  - Criteria: resident in or very close to the area, respected by different groups (youth, elderly, different ethnicities), good listening skills, no ongoing addiction or serious crime (but previous convictions do not disqualify – on the contrary, lived experience can be an asset).
  - References from local associations, religious communities, sports clubs.

**Training** (200 hours distributed over 10 weeks or equivalent):

| Module | Content | Hours |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Conflict theory | Understanding conflict dynamics, the staircase of violence | 20 |
| Mediation techniques | Active listening, rephrasing, creating agreements | 40 |
| Child and youth rights | Laws concerning children, mandatory reporting, confidentiality | 20 |
| De-escalation | Handling threatening situations, avoiding violence | 20 |
| Cultural competence | Different norms, honor-related issues, religious aspects | 20 |
| Law | What are you allowed to do? When must the police be contacted? | 20 |
| Practicum | 60 hours of fieldwork supervised by an experienced mediator | 60 |
| **Total** | | **200** |

**Organizational model**:

  - The mediator is employed by the municipality, a non-profit association, or a cooperative – but must be independent from the police to maintain trust.
  - Each mediator has a **supervisor** (experienced mediator or psychologist/social worker) for regular reflection and support.
  - The mediator reports to a **mediation center** (municipal or regional) that ensures quality, assigns cases, and handles difficult situations.
  - The mediator wears **no uniform** and carries no weapon. Their authority comes from the person, not from the state.

**Evaluation**:

  - Number of mediations per mediator per month (target: 5–10 active conflicts)
  - Proportion of mediations leading to an agreement (target: \>80%)
  - Proportion of agreements that hold after 3 months (follow-up)
  - Change in the number of shootings, assaults, group violence in the area (compared to a control area)
  - Surveys to youth, parents, and local actors regarding perceived security and trust

| **Total first year** | **SEK 56.6 million** |
| **Annual operation after scale-up** | **SEK 46.6 million** (excl. training) |

**Financing**: Compared to the police budget (SEK 35 billion) or social services' costs for youth who commit crimes (several billion), SEK 56 million is a very small investment. If the mediators prevent just 10 serious shootings a year, they have saved society several hundred million kronor.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Social Services Act (2001:453)** – add a section: *"The social welfare committee may engage or employ community mediators to prevent and manage conflicts in residential areas. The mediator's task is to mediate in disputes that do not require police intervention."*

2.  **The Secrecy Act** – the mediator must be able to promise confidentiality for parties to dare to speak. Introduce an exemption in the Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act (OSL) that protects the mediator's professional secrecy, with the same exceptions as for lawyers and psychologists (mandatory reporting upon suspicion of serious crimes against children remains).

3.  **Act on Mediation Procedures** – a simple framework law defining what a mediation agreement is, how it is documented, and stating that it is not legally binding but can be invoked in court as evidence of the parties' intentions.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is letting criminals into the system – former gang members can continue their activities."

**Answer**: Recruitment must be meticulous. A previously convicted person who has left crime behind can be the most credible mediator for youth at risk. But background checks, probationary employment, and close supervision are required. The risk of relapse is small if the person has a meaningful job and social support.

### "The police will feel bypassed – mediators are taking their jobs."

**Answer**: The mediator's job is to make the police's job possible. When conflicts are resolved early, the burden on the police decreases. And for serious crimes, the police are still the only ones with a monopoly on violence. Mediators and police can collaborate: the mediator builds trust, the police investigate crimes.

### "200 hours of training is not enough – mediation is a profession."

**Answer**: 200 hours is more than what most volunteer mediators have (often 40–80 hours). It is less than a social worker degree (3 years), but the mediator does not have the same responsibilities – they will not conduct investigations or make decisions on compulsory care. 200 hours is enough to learn basic mediation techniques and de-escalation, especially combined with a practicum.

### "Is there a risk that the mediator themselves will be subjected to violence?"

**Answer**: Yes, there is some risk. Therefore, the mediator should never work alone in high-risk areas, always have an alarm function, and have access to supervision. Employer insurance covers injuries. But the risk is lower than for police officers, because the mediator does not wear a uniform and acts as a neutral party – not as an authority figure.

### "Are there any international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes. The UK's "Violence Interrupters" (a model from Cure Violence, Chicago) uses former gang members to mediate conflicts – it has been shown to reduce shootings by 40–70% in pilot areas. Sweden already has small-scale examples (e.g., "Sluta skjut" in Malmö). Our model scales up and professionalizes this.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Sluta skjut (Stop Shooting) (Malmö)** – a gang intervention method that uses mediators from civil society, but is limited to acute conflicts. Our model is preventive and broader.
  - **Field assistants** – many municipalities have field workers who move among youth, but their mediation mandate is often unclear and underfunded. Community mediators are a specialized, better-trained version.
  - **Mediation centers (Victim-Offender Mediation)** – have existed since 2008 for young offenders, but are reactive (after a crime). Community mediators are proactive (before a crime).
  - **Norway's "Konfliktråd" (Conflict Councils)** – Norway has local conflict councils with mediators. Sweden can copy and adapt this.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal politician or an official in social services**: Identify 2–3 people in your area who already act as informal mediators (an imam, a football coach, an engaged grandmother). Ask them if they would like training and a small compensation. Start a mini-pilot.

**If you are an association leader or religious leader**: Offer to be a test pilot for the training module. Help recruit candidates from your network.

**If you are a researcher or evaluator**: Contact an interested municipality and offer to follow a pilot with a randomized control group. Evidence of impact is crucial for scaling up.
