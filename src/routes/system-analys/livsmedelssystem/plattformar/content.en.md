# Digital Infrastructure for Direct Contact

[← Back to main article](..)

**Summary**: REKO shows the demand exists—but Facebook groups don't scale. This section describes a federated, open platform built on Swish and BankID, with a cooperative ownership model that makes buyouts and co-optation structurally impossible.

## The Measure

Open-source platforms that enable direct contact between producer and consumer, cooperative coordination, and transparent origin tracking. This breaks the retail oligopoly by creating alternative distribution channels—not by competing on price, but by offering relationships, traceability, and local adaptation.

## Swedish Model – The REKO Ring

REKO (*Rättvis Konsumtion* / Fair Consumption) is a movement where consumers and producers meet via local Facebook groups. Orders are placed digitally, and delivery takes place at a shared time and location. REKO has already shown that Swedes want to buy directly—but the model is manual and scales poorly. Our measure builds on REKO's idea by providing **open and automated infrastructure** that handles payment, identity, logistics, and warehousing, without a platform owner being able to take control or change the rules.

## Technical Architecture

Federated rather than centralized platforms. Each municipality or cooperative can run its own instance (like its own website), but all instances talk to each other via a shared, open protocol. This means that a farmer in Dalarna can sell to a consumer in Skåne without being connected to the same platform provider. Interoperability without central control—exactly how email works.

## Onboarding Strategy – How Do We Get People to Use It?

**1. Start with public procurement as the driving force**: Municipalities that have already introduced 25% local procurement require their suppliers to use the platform for reporting and invoicing. This way, farmers aren't forced in—they are given a business reason.

**2. Build on existing REKO groups**: Offer group administrators a smoother transition from manual Facebook posts to automated orders. Many REKO producers are already technically curious—they will become early adopters.

**3. Integrate with Swish and BankID**: Payment takes place directly between the consumer and the producer (no platform holds the money). BankID guarantees that both parties are real people/companies, which builds security without an intermediary.

**4. Municipal "food hubs" as physical nodes**: In each pilot municipality, an existing location (a community center, library, or a farmer's yard) is designated where goods are collected and picked up. The platform shows the physical path of every product—from soil to hub.

## Why Would Producers and Consumers Switch from ICA?

Not switch—complement. The platform does not claim to replace grocery retail for all goods. It creates a **parallel track** for local, seasonal, and traceable products that currently do not reach the market because they do not fit the volume and logistics requirements of retail. For the consumer: lower prices (no middleman), greater knowledge of origin, and a relationship with the farmer. For the producer: higher margins and immediate payment.

## Risks and Responses to Criticism

- *"No one will bother with a new app."* – That is why we are building on Swish and BankID, which are already installed on every Swedish phone. The interface can be as simple as an SMS or a web form.
- *"ICA will copy the concept."* – Let them. A centralized platform from ICA can never offer the same transparency and local adaptation as a federated, open solution. A cooperative ownership model means that the incentives will not be to maximize extraction, but to minimize friction for the members.

## Co-optation Resistance

ICA, Coop, and Axfood will not fight back openly. They will launch their own "local" labels, start platforms for direct sales, and use their market influence to neutralize the reform without changing the underlying structure.

**The mechanism of co-optation**:

1. **Symbolic adaptation** – The chains create their own "REKO-like" features in their apps, but dictate the terms (pricing, logistics, data extraction).
2. **Regulatory capture** – Industry organizations lobby to make the definition of "local food" so broad that even centrally produced goods qualify.
3. **Buyouts** – When an open platform begins to grow, the founders are offered a sum they cannot refuse.

**How to build co-optation-resistant systems**:

- **Ownership structure that locks in the mission**: Platforms and cooperatives should be organized as **economic associations with limited dividend rights** or as **foundations** where the bylaws require a two-thirds majority to change the purpose. This makes a buyout legally impossible or pointless.
- **Open standards, not open source alone**: Open source is not enough if the platform is run by a single company. The requirement is a **federated architecture** (like email) where no single actor can shut down or take over the network.
- **Statutory definition of "local"**: Preempt the industry's dilution of the concept by pushing through a legal definition (e.g., within 50 km or the same bioregion) in public procurement.
- **Member-owned, not user-owned**: Cooperative models with "one member – one vote" and the right of first refusal for members in the event of a sale make buyouts practically impossible.

**Examples from Sweden**: Lantmännen started as a cooperative but has over time become increasingly centralized and market-oriented. This shows that a cooperative form is not a guarantee—the bylaws must be specifically written to counteract co-optation. Lessons from the Swedish Tourist Association (STF) and the Cooperative Union (KF) during the 20th century show both potential and pitfalls.

Resistance will not primarily come as open conflict, but as an invitation to "collaboration" that hollows out the content of the reform. Therefore, every measure must specify not only *how* it is implemented, but also *what form of ownership and governance makes it co-optation-resistant*.

## Integration With Other Measures

The platform is connected to mobile slaughterhouses, mills, and fermentation units (see [processing infrastructure](../foradling)) so that small producers can coordinate logistics and capacity. It becomes the backbone of a distributed food system—not a new centralized marketplace.
