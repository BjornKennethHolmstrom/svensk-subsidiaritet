# Security centers

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Transform fortress-like police stations into integrated security centers—open, welcoming hubs where the police are part of a larger whole. A security center contains a police reception (max 30% of the area), mediation rooms, youth activities, legal help, mental health care, social services, and coordination of neighborhood watch. The staffing consists of 70% community workers (mediators, field workers, counselors, security guides) and 30% police officers. The goal: break the residents' distrust, create natural meeting places for informal intelligence exchange, and shift the focus from reactive crime fighting to preventive security building.

## The mechanism

Today, police stations are often inaccessible, intimidating, and located in the periphery. In many vulnerable areas, the stations have been closed completely (Rosengård, Rinkeby, Bergsjön). Residents see the police as a distant, repressive force—not as a resource for security.

**The measure**: Convert existing police stations—or open new, smaller units—into **security centers** with the following design principles:

  - **Open floor plan**: Entrance without security checks (except to the police workspaces). Cafe, seating, public restrooms. Doors are open during the daytime, evening hours at least three days a week.
  - **Mixed staffing**: Police in uniform are visible, but they are a minority. The majority are community workers in civilian clothes—mediators, security guides, counselors, youth leaders, lawyers. They can be identified by a clear name tag, but no uniform.
  - **Clear functions**:
      - *Police reception* (small) – to report crimes, leave tips, pick up passports. Separated from other areas for privacy.
      - *Mediation rooms* – safe, neutral rooms for conflict mediation, restorative justice, conversations between parties.
      - *Youth area* – billiards, computers, games, sofas. Staffed by youth leaders or field workers. Open after school hours.
      - *Legal advice* – drop-in with lawyers (non-profit or municipal) who help with leases, benefits, debts.
      - *Social services* – present on site at least two days a week. Meetings can be booked.
      - *Security guide* – a person who coordinates all actors, knows the resources in the area, and can guide residents correctly.
      - *Neighborhood area* – bookable room for associations, village communities, security rings. Can be used for meetings, coffee, activities.
  - **Visible police presence without threat**: The police workspaces are separated but visible through glass walls. Residents see police working—it demystifies and creates trust. The police can also have an "open office" a few hours a week where anyone can stop by and talk.

**How it creates security**:

| Problem today | Solution with security center |
|--------------|------------------------------|
| Residents don't dare go to the police station | The center is open, welcoming, mixed staffing – the threshold is lowered |
| The police have no local knowledge | The center is staffed by community workers who know the area and can guide the police right |
| Youth have no place to be | Youth area with adult role models – prevents recruitment |
| Conflicts escalate to violence | Mediation rooms are available immediately – no waiting time |
| Residents don't know where to turn | The security guide coordinates all actors – one entry point |

## Pilot design – 10 centers first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 2-4)**: Ten existing or former police stations (or other municipal premises) in vulnerable areas are transformed into security centers. The selection is made in collaboration between the Swedish Police Authority, affected municipalities, and civil society.

**Possible pilot locations** (examples – can be varied):

| Location | Status today | Potential |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Rosengård, Malmö | Station closed 2018 | Reopen as a center – symbolically important |
| Rinkeby, Stockholm | Station closed? (partially open) | Rebuild into a center |
| Bergsjön, Gothenburg | Small police presence, no station | New establishment in existing municipal premises |
| Södertälje (Hovsjö) | Police presence but closed station | Transform |
| Botkyrka (Alby) | Former station, now other activity | Reclaim and rebuild |
| Norrköping (Hageby) | Small police station | Expand with community areas |
| Västerås (Bäckby) | Police presence, no station | New establishment |
| Helsingborg (Drottninghög) | Station exists, but is closed | Open up |
| Uppsala (Gottsunda) | Police presence, no station | New establishment in existing premises |
| Gävle (Andersberg) | Small police station | Expand |

**Organizational model**:

  - **Property owner**: The Swedish Police Authority (for existing stations) or the municipality (for new establishments). The premises are rented out to the center's operations at cost price.
  - **Principal**: A foundation or cooperative that includes the Swedish Police Authority, the municipality, and civil society representatives (e.g., local associations, Victim Support Sweden). The board is appointed by these parties (one-third each).
  - **Financing**: Operations are financed through a mix of police funds (for the police section), municipal funds (for social services, youth activities, guides), and state grants (for mediation, legal advice).
  - **Staff**: Police officers (investigators, community police)—financed by the Swedish Police Authority. Community workers—financed by the municipality, region, or non-profit organizations.

**Dimensioning keys** (for a center serving 20,000 residents):

| Function | Area (sqm) | Staff (full-time equivalents) |
|----------|-----------|----------------------------|
| Police reception and office | 150 | 4 police officers (community police, investigators) |
| Mediation rooms (2) | 40 | 2 mediators (half-time) |
| Youth area | 100 | 2 youth leaders |
| Legal advice | 20 | 0.5 lawyer (non-profit + municipal) |
| Social services | 30 | 1 social secretary (part-time) |
| Security guide | 15 | 1 guide |
| Neighborhood area | 50 | (booked by external parties) |
| Cafe/meeting place | 80 | 1 host (part-time) |
| Administration, tech | 50 | 1 coordinator |
| **Total** | **535 sqm** | **approx. 12 full-time equivalents** |

**Evaluation**:

  - Number of visitors per week (target: \>1,000 on average)
  - Proportion of visitors who state they feel safer after visiting (survey scale)
  - Number of tips to the police that come via the center (compared to previously)
  - Change in perceived security in the area (security survey before/after)
  - Change in the number of shootings, bombings, assaults (trends)

## Budget and financing

| Item | One-time cost (per center) | Annual operation (per center) |
|------|----------------------------|--------------------------|
| Rebuilding (535 sqm, medium standard) | SEK 5–10 million | – |
| Furnishings, equipment (furniture, computers, alarms) | SEK 1 million | – |
| Police staff (4 × SEK 1.2 million) | – | SEK 4.8 million |
| Community staff (8 × SEK 0.5 million average) | – | SEK 4.0 million |
| Rent, operations (electricity, cleaning, security) | – | SEK 1.5 million |
| Administration, coordination | – | SEK 1.0 million |
| **Total per center** | **SEK 6–11 million** | **SEK 11.3 million** |
| **Total 10 centers** | **SEK 60–110 million** | **SEK 113 million/year** |

**Financing**: Compared to the police budget (SEK 35 billion) and the municipalities' social services budgets (over SEK 100 billion), SEK 113 million/year is a small investment. If just one center in Rosengård reduces shootings by 10%, it has paid for itself many times over. The one-time cost can be financed through the reallocation of the police's investment funds or the municipalities' real estate budgets.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Police Ordinance (2014:1104)** – add a section: *"The Police Authority may, together with the municipality and civil society, establish security centers where police operations are co-located with social services, mediation, youth activities, and legal advice."*
2.  **The Secrecy Act** – to enable information exchange between police, social services, and mediators within the center, introduce an exemption in the OSL (Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act) for "collaboration within the framework of a security center". Secrecy is only broken to prevent serious crime or acute danger.
3.  **Exceptions to the Tenancy Act** – when the municipality or police rent out to non-profit associations at cost price or a subsidized price, they are exempted from the Public Procurement Act (LOU) because it is "internal collaboration" rather than procurement.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "The police shouldn't be running youth centers – it's not their job."

**Answer**: The police make up only 30% of the staff in the center. The main responsibility for the youth area, mediation, and legal advice lies with community workers, not police officers. The role of the police is to be available for urgent matters and build relationships—not to run leisure activities.

### "This is too expensive – we can't afford it."

**Answer**: SEK 113 million per year for 10 centers is less than 0.3% of the police budget. And compared to the costs of gang violence (SEK 200+ billion/year), it is an extremely profitable investment if the centers contribute to reducing violence. Furthermore, existing premises can be used—in many areas, there are empty police stations and municipal premises.

### "The police union will protest – police shouldn't collaborate with 'civilians'."

**Answer**: The union has been critical of the centralization that led to rotating duties and the loss of local anchoring. Security centers offer an opportunity for police to work on relationship-building in a team with other professionals—many police officers are asking for exactly this. It could become a recruitment advantage.

### "Residents don't trust the police – they won't come to a center with police officers."

**Answer**: That is why only 30% of the staff are police officers. The entrance has no security checks, and one can visit the youth area, legal advice, or cafe without ever meeting a police officer. Trust is built gradually. Experiences from similar centers in the UK ("Police and Communities Together" – PACT) show that visits increase over time.

### "Are there international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes. The UK's "One Stop Shop"—integrated service centers with police, social services, and housing companies. The US's "Community Service Centers" in Chicago and New York. The closest example is Norway's "Trygghetssenter" in Oslo (Tøyen)—open since 2019 with good results. Sweden can learn and do better.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Previous attempts with local police stations** – before 2015, there were local stations with some collaboration. Security centers are an upgraded, more integrated version.
  - **Citizen offices** – many municipalities have citizen offices where several authorities are co-located. Security centers expand this to police and mediation.
  - **Field workers and youth clinics** – exist in many areas, but often separately from the police. Co-location creates synergies.
  - **Norway's "Tøyen trygghetssenter"** – established in 2019 in a vulnerable area in Oslo. Contains police, social services, youth club, legal help. Evaluation shows increased security and reduced crime. Sweden should visit and copy this.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal commissioner or police chief**: Book a study trip to Tøyen trygghetssenter in Oslo. Meet their staff. See how it works. Come home and propose a pilot in your municipality.

**If you are a property owner in a vulnerable area**: Donate or rent out a space at cost price to start a small security center in mini-format—a reception, a mediation room, a youth area. Start small.

**If you are a local association leader**: Write a letter to the police and the municipality: "We want a security center in our area. Here is a space we can use. Here are associations that can contribute staff." Show that the demand exists.
