# Cultural commons

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Transform 100 municipal cultural centers from professionally programmed institutions into community-controlled cooperatives (one member, one vote). 70% of the program is determined by local groups, 30% professionally curated. Resources (equipment, rooms, expertise) available to all members. The goal: break professional gatekeepers' monopoly over cultural expression and let cultural centers become toolboxes for residents' own creation—not stages for established actors.

## The mechanism

Today, most municipal cultural centers are governed by officials and political committees. The program is determined by employed curators who book professional artists, exhibitors, and lecturers. Residents are passive consumers. The result: the same type of offering as in all other municipalities—standardized, safe, Stockholm-inspired.

**The measure**: Transform the cultural center into a **cultural commons**—a legal and organizational form where:

  - The center is managed by a **cooperative** where everyone who uses the center can become a member (one member, one vote)
  - **70% of the program budget** is determined by the members through member meetings or digital voting
  - **30%** is reserved for professionally curated content (to retain quality-enhancing edge and invited guests)
  - **Equipment** (camera, lighting, sound, workshop machines) is available for all members to borrow, often for free or at cost price
  - **Rooms** can be booked by members for their own events free of charge (except for cleaning)

**What it enables**:

  - A group of Somali women can book the main stage for a dance gathering without going through curators
  - A teenager interested in rap can borrow professional recording equipment for a demo
  - A pensioner's association can show their own film in the cinema
  - Associations can collaborate on a theme night without paying expensive rent

## Pilot design – ten cultural centers first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: Ten municipal cultural centers in different parts of the country are transformed into cultural commons. The selection is based on the municipality's willingness, the size of the center, and the composition of the population.

### Pilot 1: Husby kulturhus (Stockholm)

  - **Current status**: Professionally governed, with a high proportion of rented program activities. Residents often feel that the center does not reflect their needs.
  - **Transformation**: Form a cooperative where everyone who lives in Järva (Husby, Akalla, Kista) can become a member (free or symbolic fee SEK 50/year). The first board is elected through a grassroots movement. The center's staff (curators, technicians) remain but get new roles—from "programmers" to "facilitators" who help members realize their own ideas.
  - **Budget**: The municipality continues to pay for operations (heating, electricity, staff)—approx. SEK 5 million/year. The program budget (SEK 1 million/year) is distributed by the members.

### Pilot 2: Blåsut kulturhus (Botkyrka)

  - **Current status**: Mixed activities, but low resident influence.
  - **Transformation**: Cooperative with a special place for language minorities. Each language group (Arabic, Kurdish, Spanish) can have its own "node" within the commons that receives a proportional share of the budget.
  - **Budget**: SEK 3 million/year from the municipality + opportunity to sell memberships (SEK 50/month) for extra resources.

### Pilot 3: Fågelviken community hall (Värmland countryside)

  - **Current status**: Small cultural center mostly used by pensioners, with low activity among younger people.
  - **Transformation**: Cooperative that also includes the community center, the school stage, and the library in a shared commons. Mobile resources (sound packages, stage lighting) that can be borrowed between towns.
  - **Budget**: SEK 0.5 million/year from the municipality + EU rural development funds.

**Evaluation after 3 years**:

  - Number of members in the cooperative (target: \>20% of residents in the catchment area)
  - Number of self-organized events per year (before: 5–10; after: \>100)
  - Proportion of the population who visited or used the center at least once a year (target: \>50%)
  - Perceived belonging and influence (survey)

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Local Government Act (2017:725)** – add a new section: *"Municipalities may transfer the management of cultural centers, libraries, or other public community premises to cooperative societies or foundations where the members are residents of the catchment area. The municipality remains the owner of the property and is responsible for basic operations."*

2.  **Agreement template** – develop a standard model for a "cultural commons agreement" between the municipality and the cooperative, with clear rules for the division of responsibilities, budget frameworks, and the right of repossession if the cooperative mismanages itself.

3.  **Exemption from rent regulation** – since members are allowed to use the rooms for free or at a low cost, this is exempted from the Act on the Leasing of Own Homes (privatuthyrningslagen). A small adjustment to avoid demarcation problems.

## Budget and financing

| Item | Annual cost (per pilot) |
|------|---------------------------|
| Operations (heating, electricity, cleaning, security) | SEK 1–5 million (depending on size) – paid by the municipality as today |
| Staff (2–5 facilitators) | SEK 1.5–4 million – the municipality or the cooperative? |
| Program budget (member-governed) | SEK 0.5–2 million – the municipality |
| Training of facilitators (first year) | SEK 100,000 per center (one-time cost) |
| Digital platform for member democracy | SEK 50,000 per center (one-time cost) |

**Financing**: Mainly municipal funds that already go to cultural centers. The difference is reallocation—from professional program purchases to member-governed activities. Net costs may decrease since members contribute with voluntary work.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "The curators will lose their jobs or be demoted."

**Answer**: No, their role changes. Instead of booking artists from Stockholm, they become facilitators who help members realize their ideas. This is more qualified work—tutoring a Somali group on using sound equipment requires pedagogy and technical knowledge, not less competence. Salaries may even increase as tasks broaden.

### "The members will only book bad amateur culture."

**Answer**: Bad in whose eyes? A group of teenagers performing their own rap songs finds it meaningful. A pensioner's association showing slides from the 1960s has high nostalgic value. The taste of professional curators is not universal. Furthermore: 30% of the budget is still professionally curated—so there is always a "quality guarantee" for those who demand it.

### "Politicians don't want to give up control."

**Answer**: They don't give up control—they delegate. The municipal council decides on frameworks (how much money, what security requirements, that operations follow laws). The cooperative decides on the content. The same model as for independent schools or housing cooperatives—proven.

### "It will be unequal – certain groups will take over."

**Answer**: The risk exists, which is why clear democratic rules are required: all votes are equal, no permanent board positions, opportunity for re-election every year. The facilitators' role is to ensure that quiet voices are also heard—through special calls, targeted funds, and language support. If one group dominates completely, the municipality can retake management after a warning.

### "Associations will outcompete individuals."

**Answer**: The commons is open to both associations and individuals. A single person can book a small room to paint—an association can book the main stage. Priority rules can be designed so that individuals get precedence at certain times. Democracy decides.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Kulturhuset Blåsut (Botkyrka)** – already experimenting with citizen dialogue today, but without a formal cooperative. Our model formalizes this.
  - **Fryshuset (Stockholm)** – run as a foundation, not a cooperative, but has a similar idea of "young people govern themselves." Could be reorganized.
  - **Bygdegårdarna (Community centers)** – traditional Swedish commons, often governed by non-profit associations. They are role models: self-government, low thresholds, local anchoring. Why can't municipal cultural centers function the same way?
  - **Library makerspaces** – equipment is already available for members to borrow today (3D printers, sewing machines). Expand the concept to the entire cultural center.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal politician**: Propose transforming one of your cultural centers into a "cultural commons" in pilot form. Use the community center model as an argument.

**If you are an official in the cultural administration**: Start on a small scale—give a local association the keys to a room once a week free of charge. Evaluate. Scale up.

**If you are a citizen**: Gather 10 people in your area and send a letter to the municipal executive board: "We want to take over the management of our local cultural center as a cooperative." Offer to be guinea pigs.
