# Welfare rings

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Groups of 10–20 households forming networks for mutual aid—early detection of needs (illness, unemployment, crisis), light support coordination (meals, childcare, companionship), and connection to professional services when truly needed. Members receive SEK 500/month in tax deductions for participating. The goal: to recreate the informal solidarity that professionalization has eroded, at a fraction of the cost of bureaucratic welfare.

## The mechanism

Today, most welfare needs are discovered only when the individual crashes and turns to professional systems—the health center, social services, the Swedish Social Insurance Agency. By then, the crisis is already a fact. Neighbors, friends, and family often see the warning signs much earlier (isolation, weight loss, missed bills) but lack the structures to act.

**The measure**: A **welfare ring** is a loose grouping of 10–20 households in the same residential area or village that:

  - Meets physically or digitally once a month (15–30 minutes)
  - Shares information about who has the capacity to help ("I can babysit on Tuesdays") and who has a need ("I need a ride to the doctor")
  - Offers light support without bureaucracy—meals, childcare, companionship, phone calls, help with simple household chores
  - Acts as an early warning mechanism—if someone in the ring is not heard from for two weeks, another member calls to check in
  - Connects to professional interventions when the need exceeds the ring's capacity (acute illness, domestic violence, severe mental illness)

**The incentive**: Each member receives a SEK 500/month tax deduction ("welfare deduction") for participating actively—attendance at meetings, reported contribution at least once a month. This recognizes that care work has value, even when not performed by professionals.

**The cost comparison**: A welfare ring with 15 households costs the state 15 × SEK 500 = SEK 7,500/month = SEK 90,000/year. For the same sum, you barely get half a social worker. The ring can deliver early detection, companionship, childcare, meals, and social cohesion—things that professional systems are very expensive and often inefficient at.

## Pilot design – 10,000 rings first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: 10,000 welfare rings in 100 municipalities, focusing on:

  - **Vulnerable areas** (according to the Police list) where both the need and the social capacity are high
  - **Rural areas** where distances to professional systems are long
  - **Senior housing** where loneliness is widespread
  - **Suburban areas with a high proportion of newly arrived immigrants** where informal networks are often weak

**Organizational model**:

  - The ring organizes itself—no application to an authority. Anyone can invite neighbors.
  - To receive the tax deduction, the ring must register with the Swedish Tax Agency (Skatteverket) with a contact person and a list of members (personal identity numbers). No requirement for bylaws or forming an association.
  - The contact person digitally certifies each month which members have been active (attended a meeting or performed a service). The Tax Agency pays the deduction automatically via the payslip.

**State support**:

  - The ring receives a SEK 2,000 start-up grant for coffee, printing, a simple website, or renting a venue.
  - The municipality provides a "ring coordinator" (1 per 100 rings) who can answer questions, share best practices, and connect to professional systems.

**Evaluation after 3 years** (compared to control areas without rings):

  - Proportion who state they "have someone to ask for help" (social capital)
  - Number of acute interventions from social services, ambulance, police (expected to decrease)
  - Self-reported loneliness and insecurity
  - Change in the municipality's costs for individual and family care

**Financing**: Compared to the costs of social services (approximately SEK 100 billion/year), SEK 950 million is less than 1%. If the rings reduce acute interventions by just 5%, they have paid for themselves several times over.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **New provision in the Income Tax Act (1999:1229)** – introduce a "welfare deduction" of SEK 500 per month for natural persons who actively participate in a registered welfare ring. The deduction is tax-free and does not affect other benefits.
2.  **Ordinance on the registration of welfare rings** – the Swedish Tax Agency is tasked with establishing a simple digital register. Requirements: contact person, address, membership list. No bureaucratic control of "quality"—the rings are allowed to organize themselves.
3.  **Exemption from the Secrecy Act** – In order for the rings to function as an early warning mechanism, members must be able to share certain information about each other (e.g., "Kalle hasn't been out of his house in a week"). Introduce an exception in the Public Access to Information and Secrecy Act (OSL 2009:400) that allows such sharing within the ring, as long as it is not spread further.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is legalizing insecurity – the state should not subsidize neighbors snooping."

**Answer**: The welfare ring is based on voluntariness and trust. No one is forced to participate. And "snooping" is the wrong word—it is about caring. In a society where everyone sits behind closed doors, loneliness is a massive problem. The welfare ring offers a structured path back to the natural care of the neighborhood.

### "Fraud – people will take the SEK 500 without doing anything."

**Answer**: The risk exists, but the ring has an incentive to police itself. If a member never participates, why would the others want to keep them? The contact person only certifies active members. And SEK 500 is enough to motivate most people, but too little to make organized fraud profitable. Random checks can be carried out.

### "The unions will protest – this threatens welfare jobs."

**Answer**: The welfare ring does not replace professional interventions—it complements them. Social workers should not cook dinner for lonely elderly people or babysit on a Tuesday evening. The ring does what professionals have neither the time nor the right competence for. On the contrary: the ring can free up professionals for what they are trained to do.

### "I don't want to be dependent on my neighbors – I want rights."

**Answer**: The rights mentality is part of the problem. A society where everyone demands professional interventions for the slightest thing is a society that has lost the ability to care. The welfare ring does not offer a legal demand for help—it offers a community. The difference is crucial. Most prefer a kind neighbor over an administrator.

### "This only works in homogeneous areas with high trust."

**Answer**: Then it is a good thing that Sweden has high trust generally. But the pilots are specifically designed to be tested in different areas—including socioeconomically vulnerable and ethnically mixed ones. Experiences from e.g., Botkyrka and Rosengård show that neighborhood networks can work even there, if they are given simple organization and modest support. If it doesn't work—then we have learned something.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Neighborhood Watch (Grannsamverkan)** (the police)—a simple model where neighbors keep an eye on each other's homes. The welfare ring extends this to care.
  - **Voluntary Resource Groups** (The Red Cross)—exist in many municipalities, but are often centrally controlled. The welfare ring is entirely self-organized.
  - **The community center movement (Bygdegårdsrörelsen)**—a historical role model for local cooperation. Less active today, but the concept lives on.
  - **"Support groups for relatives"**—exist within elderly care, but often require professional leadership. The welfare ring is more horizontal.

No Swedish model does exactly this—but fragments exist. The welfare ring gathers them into a simple, scalable idea.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal politician**: Submit a budget request for SEK 100,000 to start a pilot with 10 welfare rings in a vulnerable area. Use the text above as a basis.

**If you are a property owner or housing company**: Put up a notice board in the stairwell: "Start a welfare ring – register your interest here." Offer a room once a month for free.

**If you are a citizen**: Invite your neighbors for a cup of coffee. Say: "I thought we could help each other out a bit—babysit, shop, call if someone is sick. No demands, just if we want to." You don't need to wait for politicians.
