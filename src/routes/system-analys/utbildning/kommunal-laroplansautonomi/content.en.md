# Municipal curriculum autonomy

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Enables municipalities to adapt up to 50% of teaching time to local needs, while national standards for basic competencies (reading, writing, arithmetic) are maintained. Each municipality develops a **local curriculum profile** that reflects the local business sector, cultural characteristics, geographical conditions, and student composition. Example: Kiruna can focus on mining technology and Sami culture, Gotland on sustainable tourism and medieval history, Helsingborg on maritime logistics and international trade. The goal: to replace today's one-size-fits-all solution with a flexible structure that follows Ashby's law—local variation requires local adaptation.

## The mechanism

Today, the Swedish curriculum (Lgr22, Gy11) is nationally uniform. A school in Kiruna and a school in Malmö are expected to cover the same content, in the same order, with the same timetable. Exceptions exist (e.g., mother tongue instruction, local additions), but they are marginal (max 10–15% of the time). This is despite the fact that the students' future labor market, cultural frames of reference, and societal challenges look completely different.

**The measure**: Introduce **municipal curriculum profiles** where:

  * Parliament establishes a **national core** (50% of teaching time) that covers basic competencies: Swedish, mathematics, English, civics, and basic natural sciences.
  * The municipalities design a **local profile** (30% of the time) that reflects local needs. Examples of content: local business sector, cultural heritage, languages, entrepreneurship, environment, technology.
  * The schools (or individual teachers) are given additional **individual flexibility** (20% of the time) to adapt to the students' interests and needs (e.g., in-depth studies, project work, internships).

**What the local profile can contain** (concrete examples):

| Municipality | Business profile | Cultural profile | Geographical profile |
|--------|------------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Kiruna | Mining technology, mountain safety, remote control of machines | Sami duodji, yoik, reindeer husbandry, Meänkieli (Tornedalian) | Arctic ecology, permafrost, space research (Esrange) |
| Gotland | Sustainable tourism, event planning, hospitality industry | Medieval Week, Gotlandic churches, limestone craftsmanship | Baltic Sea ecology, coastal farming, wind power |
| Helsingborg | Maritime logistics, international trade, port operations | Oresund region, cross-border collaboration | Marine biology, urban development in Oresund |
| Värmland | Forestry, paper pulp, renewable energy | Storytelling tradition (Selma Lagerlöf), Värmlandic dialect | Forest ecology, hydropower, wild reindeer |
| Botkyrka | Social entrepreneurship, diversity entrepreneurship | Language immersion (Arabic, Somali, Spanish), suburban culture | Urban agriculture, recycling, environmental technology |

**How it works in practice**:

1.  The municipal council appoints a **curriculum committee** with teachers, principals, business representatives, cultural creators, students, and parents.
2.  The committee produces a proposal for a local profile (goals, content, time allocation) within the framework of the national core.
3.  The Swedish National Agency for Education quality-assures that the profile does not violate national minimum requirements (e.g., that Swedish or mathematics are not removed).
4.  The Swedish Schools Inspectorate follows up that the profile is implemented and that students reach the knowledge goals.
5.  After 4 years, the profile is evaluated nationally—successful profiles can be spread, unsuccessful ones dismantled.

**The cybernetic point**: Today's uniform curriculum violates Ashby's law—it has too low variation to match Sweden's different municipalities. Municipal profiling increases variation (higher "variety") and thus the system's ability to adapt to the local context.

## Pilot design – 5 municipalities first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-5)**: Five municipalities representing different parts of Sweden are granted an exemption from 50% of the curriculum requirements to develop and test local profiles.

**Proposed pilot municipalities** (according to the examples above):

1.  **Kiruna** – sparsely populated area, Sami culture, mining industry, space technology
2.  **Gotland** – island community, tourism, medieval history, coastal ecology
3.  **Helsingborg** – big city, logistics, international trade, Oresund
4.  **Värmland** – forest region, paper industry, storytelling tradition
5.  **Botkyrka** – diversity, suburb, social entrepreneurship, urban agriculture

**Process for each pilot municipality**:

  * Year 1: The municipality forms a curriculum committee and produces a proposal for a local profile.
  * Year 2: The Swedish National Agency for Education approves the profile (adjustments may be required). Preparations in the schools (teacher training, materials).
  * Years 3–4: Implementation in all schools in the municipality (or a selection of schools). Data collection (student results, motivation, teacher experience).
  * Year 5: Evaluation and report. Decision on continuation or national scale-up.

**Evaluation criteria**:

  * National test results in the core subjects (Swedish, mathematics, English)—must not deteriorate compared to control municipalities.
  * Students' motivation and attendance (measured with surveys, less unexcused absence).
  * Transition to work or higher education (proportion who have a job or are studying within 6 months after upper secondary school).
  * Employers' satisfaction (survey to local companies about new employees' competence).
  * Teachers' experience of autonomy and job satisfaction (rating scale).

## Budget and financing

| Item | Amount (per pilot municipality) | Total 5 municipalities |
|------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Curriculum committee (20 people × 10 meetings × fee SEK 2,000) | SEK 400,000 | SEK 2,000,000 |
| Teacher training (2 days × 500 teachers × SEK 1,500/day) | SEK 1,500,000 | SEK 7,500,000 |
| Development of local teaching materials (digital, open) | SEK 500,000 | SEK 2,500,000 |
| Evaluation (researchers, data collection, report) | SEK 800,000 | SEK 4,000,000 |
| Administration and coordination | SEK 300,000 | SEK 1,500,000 |
| **Total per municipality** | **SEK 3,500,000** | **SEK 17,500,000** |

**Financing**: SEK 17.5 million over 5 years = SEK 3.5 million per year. This is less than 0.01% of the school budget (approx. SEK 300 billion/year). The money can be taken from the Swedish National Agency for Education's development funds or the state grant for equitable schooling. Socio-economically: if the pilots lead to a better match between education and the labor market (lower unemployment, higher productivity), the investment is self-financing many times over.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Education Act (2010:800)** – add a new section: *"The government may, upon application, grant a municipality an exemption from the regulations concerning the curriculum's timetable to test a local curriculum profile. The exemption may cover a maximum of 50% of the teaching time. National knowledge requirements in Swedish, mathematics, and English must always be met."*
2.  **Amendment to the Upper Secondary School Ordinance (2010:2039)** – corresponding exemptions for the upper secondary school's program structure.
3.  **Ordinance on a national core** – the government determines which subjects and knowledge requirements must always be included (minimum level). The remaining time is flexible.
4.  **The Swedish National Agency for Education's regulations** – adjusted so that municipal profiles can be approved in a simple, transparent process (max 6 months processing time).

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This threatens equivalence—students in different municipalities get different knowledge."

**Answer**: Equivalence is not about identical content, but about equal opportunities. A student in Kiruna today has worse opportunities to learn mining technology or Sami culture than a student in Stockholm—even though this knowledge is highly relevant to Kiruna's labor market. The national core guarantees basic competencies. The local profile provides actual equivalence—the opportunity to learn what is relevant in one's context.

### "The Swedish National Agency for Education will say no—they are conservative."

**Answer**: Therefore, political governance is required. The government can task the Swedish National Agency for Education with developing a process for municipal profiles. And the pilots are voluntary—no one is forced. When the Swedish National Agency for Education sees that results do not deteriorate (on the contrary, motivation increases), the resistance will decrease. Experiences from Finnish equivalents (local curricula since 2004) show that central authorities can adapt.

### "Teachers already have too much to do—developing local profiles will be a new administrative burden."

**Answer**: The curriculum profile is developed by a committee with allocated time (not unpaid evening work). The municipality can hire a curriculum strategist. And once the profile is developed, the administrative burden for teachers decreases—they do not have to adapt a Stockholm-centered curriculum to local conditions. It is extra work initially, but long-term relief.

### "What happens if a municipality makes a bad profile—the students will suffer?"

**Answer**: The pilot has clear quality requirements and evaluation. If a profile does not work (deteriorated results in core subjects, low goal attainment), the exemption is revoked. The risk is small—municipalities have an incentive to do a good job. And today's one-size-fits-all solution is already bad for many municipalities—the status quo is not risk-free.

### "Are there international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, several. **Finland** has national curriculum foundations (like our national core) and municipal curricula that specify local priorities—this has worked since 2004. **Norway** has a similar model. **Canada** (the provinces) has large local variation. Sweden is unusually centralized compared to its Nordic neighbors. We are lagging behind—it is time to follow suit.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  * **Local work plans** (existed before 1994) – municipalities previously had greater freedom. Reintroduce in a modern form.
  * **Mother tongue instruction** – already an example of local adaptation today (the municipality decides which languages are offered). The scale model exists.
  * **National minority languages** – schools in Sami administrative areas already have an expanded mission. Can be extended to other local profiles.
  * **Finnish municipal curricula** – each Finnish municipality has its own curriculum within the framework of national foundations. Sweden can copy the organization.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal politician**: Submit a proposal that the municipality should apply for pilot status for a local curriculum profile. Gather broad political support (all parties win on local adaptation). Use the text above as a basis.

**If you are a teacher or principal**: Start a discussion at your school: "What would we do differently if we got to decide 50% of the time?" Gather ideas. Send them to your municipal executive board.

**If you are a business or cultural representative**: Contact the municipality and offer to participate in a curriculum committee. Explain what competencies are in demand in your industry. Make your voice heard.
