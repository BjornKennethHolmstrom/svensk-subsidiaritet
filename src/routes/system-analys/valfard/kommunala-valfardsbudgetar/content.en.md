# Municipal welfare budgets

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Municipalities receive a consolidated welfare budget per capita (based on socio-economic needs) instead of earmarked government grants and minutely regulated programs. National minimum levels guarantee that no one falls below the poverty line, but methods, priorities, and organization are determined locally. The goal: allow different municipalities to test different solutions—basic income, service vouchers, community grants, social investment funds—and create a Swedish evidence base for what actually works in different local contexts.

## The mechanism

Today, Swedish welfare is governed by a complex mix of national laws, ordinances, regulations, and earmarked government grants. Municipalities are responsible for implementation but have very little freedom to adapt methods to local conditions. A municipality with high unemployment and a municipality with an aging population are forced to use the same program templates—even though the needs are fundamentally different.

**The measure**: Introduce **municipal welfare budgets**—a reform in three steps:

1.  **Merge earmarked government grants** (income support, housing allowance, elderly care, disability support, labor market interventions, etc.) into a **one-dimensional needs-based transfer** per municipality.
2.  **Remove detailed regulations**—national goals ("reduce child poverty," "increase the proportion in work") remain, but the methods to achieve the goals are determined by the municipal council.
3.  **Introduce evaluation and accountability**—each municipality must annually report how the budget was used and what results were achieved. In case of repeated failures, the state can revoke the delegation (sanction).

**What the municipality is allowed to do** (examples of local variations):

| Municipality type | Possible solution | Why locally adapted? |
|-----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| Large city with high unemployment (Malmö, Södertälje) | Basic income or job guarantee for young people | Traditional measures have failed, need for radical tests |
| Sparsely populated municipality with aging population (Älvdalen, Dorotea) | Service vouchers for the elderly (choose your own home care, food box, neighbor help) | Long distances, few private alternatives—empower the elderly |
| Suburban municipality with high segregation (Botkyrka, Rinkeby-Kista) | Community grants to local associations that run employment activities | Professional systems do not reach people—associations do |
| University city (Uppsala, Lund) | Investment support for cooperative student housing and psychosocial support | Young adults fall through the cracks—flexible solutions are needed |

**National minimum levels** (guarantees that no municipality may fall below):

  - No person shall have a disposable income below the national standard for income support (unless other support is available)
  - Elderly care shall guarantee a minimum level of service (exact level determined nationally)
  - Children in economically vulnerable households shall have access to at least one organized leisure activity
  - Emergency social services (domestic violence, homelessness, addiction) must be available every day

**Evaluation and learning**:

  - A national knowledge center (lifted from the National Board of Health and Welfare) collects data from all municipalities, analyzes what works (and what does not), and spreads best practices.
  - Each municipality must budget at least 0.5% of the welfare budget for independent evaluation.
  - After 8 years, a statutory review is conducted: which local models should be scaled up nationally? Which should be abandoned?

## Pilot design – 20 municipalities first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-4)**: 20 municipalities (representative of different municipality types) receive a delegated welfare budget with exemptions from detailed regulations. The pilot municipalities sign an agreement with the government specifying national minimum levels and evaluation requirements.

**Selection criteria**:

  - Geographical distribution (northern, central, southern Sweden)
  - Varying population base (large city, medium-sized city, rural area, sparsely populated area)
  - Varying socio-economic challenges (unemployment, aging, segregation, integration problems)
  - Political will—the municipality must apply and show that it has the capacity to implement the reform

**Budget model**:

  - Each pilot municipality receives a **per capita compensation** based on:
      - Number of inhabitants (base)
      - Socio-economic needs index (unemployment, education level, ill health, proportion of foreign-born)
      - Geographical disadvantages (sparseness, long distances)
  - The compensation replaces all previous government grants for the welfare areas (income support, elderly care, disability, labor market, childcare? – boundaries to be discussed). The municipality retains its tax capacity unchanged.
  - The compensation is adjusted annually for inflation and population changes.

**Examples of annual compensation (the pilots)**:

| Municipality | Type | Inhabitants | Needs index | Compensation (SEK million) | Compensation per capita (SEK) |
|--------|-----|----------|-------------|------------------|---------------------------|
| Malmö | Large city, high unemployment | 350,000 | 1.25 | 4,375 | 12,500 |
| Dorotea | Sparsely populated, aging | 1,300 | 1.80 | 23 | 17,700 |
| Botkyrka | Suburb, segregation | 95,000 | 1.35 | 1,282 | 13,500 |
| Uppsala | University city | 240,000 | 0.95 | 2,280 | 9,500 |

**Evaluation** (independent researchers):

  - Compare pilot municipalities with matched control municipalities (which retain traditional governance)
  - Outcome measures: unemployment, child poverty, quality of life for the elderly, homelessness, cost development per inhabitant
  - Interviews with citizens, users, administrators, and politicians
  - Annual reports are published and audited by the Swedish National Audit Office (Riksrevisionen)

## Budget and financing

| Item | Annual cost (20 pilot municipalities) |
|------|----------------------------------|
| Compensation to the municipalities (based on current government grants + tax equalization, no change in total level) | SEK 0 (reallocation) |
| Evaluation (research, data collection) | SEK 15 million |
| National knowledge center (lifted from the National Board of Health and Welfare) | SEK 10 million |
| Municipalities' own evaluation costs (0.5% of budget) | approx. SEK 30 million (total) |
| **Total first year** | **SEK 55 million** |

**Financing**: Within existing government grants for welfare (approximately SEK 200 billion annually). SEK 55 million is 0.03%—a negligible proportion to test a potentially transformative reform.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the Social Services Act (2001:453)** – add a new section: *"The government may, upon application, grant a municipality an exemption from the detailed regulations in this act to test alternative forms of welfare provision. The exemption may cover a maximum of 20 municipalities and must be evaluated after four years."*
2.  **Amendment to the Act on Government Grants to Municipalities** – replace earmarked grants with a needs-based flat rate for the pilot municipalities.
3.  **Ordinance on national minimum levels** – the government establishes which guarantees all municipalities (including pilots) must meet, e.g., national standard for income support, minimum staffing in elderly care.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "Municipalities will lower services to save money."

**Answer**: National minimum levels prevent a race to the bottom. And since the compensation is needs-based, municipalities have no incentive to skimp—on the contrary, if they deliver poor results, it shows in the evaluation and risks the state revoking the delegation. Most municipalities want to do a good job, not minimize costs.

### "Inequality between municipalities will increase."

**Answer**: The current system is already deeply unequal—the differences in the quality of elderly care between rich and poor municipalities are enormous. Our model compensates for socio-economic needs, which reduces inequality. And by allowing different solutions, poor municipalities can test innovative, cost-effective methods that are currently prohibited.

### "The state loses control and cannot guarantee equivalence."

**Answer**: Equivalence is not the same as uniformity. Two municipalities can reach the same goals (low unemployment, satisfied elderly care) using completely different methods. The state's role is to set goals and evaluate results—not to dictate methods. This is called trust-based management, and it has been an official policy for several years without being implemented. Now we are making it a reality.

### "Bureaucrats and trade unions will oppose it—they lose influence."

**Answer**: Some will do that. But many municipal officials are frustrated by today's regulatory hassle and would welcome greater room to maneuver. Trade unions can gain from their members getting more qualified, varied tasks instead of being form-fillers. The transition must be handled carefully, with training and clear career paths.

### "Are there any international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, several. The UK's "local authority flexibility" (some delegation, but incomplete). Finland's "social welfare pilot regions" (positive experiences). Canada's "Ontario Works integration" (mixed results, but learning). Sweden's strong municipal self-government is a unique foundation—we can go further than other countries.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **Municipal self-government (the constitution)** – Sweden already has a unique position with strong municipalities. This is underutilized today.
  - **Delegation trials in elderly care** – several municipalities have tested "free years," "short-term housing," "user influence" with good results. But they have had to fight against national rules.
  - **LOV (Act on System of Choice in the Public Sector)** – gives municipalities the right to introduce freedom of choice in care. The same logic could be extended to the entire welfare sector.
  - **Norway's "free municipality trials" (frikommuneforsøk)** – Norway has had trials with deregulated municipalities since the 1980s. Experiences are overwhelmingly positive (more innovation, more satisfied users). Sweden is lagging behind—time to catch up.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal commissioner or municipal director**: Send a letter to the government and SALAR (Swedish Association of Local Authorities and Regions) expressing interest in becoming a pilot municipality in a welfare budget reform. Gather commissioners from several parties—the broader the political support, the better.

**If you are a Member of Parliament or state secretary**: Present a bill for a legislative amendment that enables pilots with municipal welfare budgets. Use the text above as a basis. Push for Sweden to become the best in the Nordic region at municipal welfare experimentation.

**If you are a researcher or evaluator**: Contact SALAR and offer to design a randomized control group study for the pilots. Good evaluation is crucial for the reform to be scaled up.
