# Social investment funds

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Community foundations that finance preventive social interventions and measure returns in reduced future benefit dependency, fewer healthcare interventions, and higher quality of life. The fund invests in projects that currently do not receive public funding because they are "preventive"—the costs are incurred now, but the savings come several years later. Through social investment funds, zero-sum thinking is broken: every million invested preventively can save several millions in future acute interventions. The goal: to create a system that rewards prevention instead of merely managing crises.

## The mechanism

Today, public budgets are divided into silos (social services, healthcare, labor market, schools) with short-term annual budgets. This creates a systemic flaw: no one has an incentive to invest in preventive measures that yield returns over several years and across multiple silos. An intervention that reduces juvenile delinquency saves money for social services, the police, prosecutors, the prison and probation service, and healthcare—but none of these authorities get to share in the savings.

**The measure**: Introduce **social investment funds**—independent community foundations that:

  - Receive start-up capital from the state, region, municipality, and/or private donors
  - Invest in preventive projects that have a high potential to reduce future public expenditures
  - Carefully measure the effects (with randomized control groups or meticulous comparisons)
  - Reinvest the savings into new preventive interventions—a self-reinforcing cycle

**What the fund can finance** (examples):

| Intervention | Target group | Expected savings | Time horizon |
|--------|----------|---------------------|--------------|
| Family therapy (home visits, parental support) | Young children in at-risk families | Reduced foster care placement (saves SEK 1–2 million/year per child) | 3–5 years |
| Supported employment (individualized job support) | Long-term sick-listed individuals with mental illness | Reduced sickness benefits, increased tax revenue | 1–2 years |
| Debt counseling + savings (individual development accounts) | Low-income earners | Reduced reliance on social assistance, increased self-sufficiency | 2–4 years |
| Building more youth activities on evenings/weekends | Youth in vulnerable areas | Reduced juvenile delinquency, reduced costs for vandalism | 1–3 years |
| Housing support + job coach (intensive package) | Newly arrived immigrants with low education | Reduced introduction benefits, faster entry to work | 2–3 years |

**The investment model**:

  - The fund grants a loan (or a grant with repayment requirements) to the project. The loan must be repaid if the expected savings are realized (according to predetermined metrics). If the project fails, the loan is written off—the risk is shared between the fund and the provider.
  - The savings are calculated by independent evaluators: comparing the costs for the target group with a control group (or historical data). The difference in public expenditures (social assistance, healthcare, prison and probation service, etc.) constitutes the "social return".
  - A portion of the savings (for example, 50%) is repaid to the fund, while the rest remains in the public sector. In this way, both the fund and the municipality/region/state get a piece of the pie.

**Example**: A family therapy intervention costs SEK 200,000. Without the intervention, there is an 80% probability that the child would be placed in a foster home for two years, costing SEK 1.6 million. With the intervention, the child is not placed in care at all. The savings for society: SEK 1.6 million – SEK 200,000 = SEK 1.4 million. The fund gets back SEK 700,000 (50%), which can be reinvested in more interventions. The municipality saves SEK 700,000.

## Pilot design – three regional funds first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-5)**: Three social investment funds are started in different parts of the country—one in a metropolitan region (e.g., Västra Götaland), one in a medium-sized region (e.g., Jönköping), and one in a sparsely populated region (e.g., Norrbotten).

**Organizational model**:

  - The fund is an **independent foundation** with a board consisting of representatives from the region, municipalities, civil society, and potential private donors.
  - Start-up capital: SEK 50 million per fund (total SEK 150 million) from the state (Ministry of Health and Social Affairs) and participating regions/municipalities (2:1 matching—the state gives SEK 2 for every SEK 1 from the region/municipality).
  - The fund hires a **Chief Investment Officer** (with experience from social work or impact investing) and a **Head of Evaluation** (statistician/economist).
  - The fund can also accept private donations or social impact bonds (if a private investor wants to join and share the risk).

**The investment process**:

1.  The fund issues a call for proposals for preventive projects. Applications are evaluated based on potential social return, scientific design, and the provider's capacity.
2.  Approved projects receive a venture capital investment (loan or conditional grant). The project must have a clear measurement plan and a control group (randomized or matched).
3.  Independent evaluators follow the project for 1–3 years and calculate the social return.
4.  Upon positive return, a predetermined share is repaid to the fund. The fund reinvests in new projects.
5.  Failed projects are written off—the fund bears the risk, but the lessons learned are shared.

**The funds' growth goals**:

  - Year 3: SEK 150 million in loaned capital, 10–20 projects
  - Year 5: SEK 300 million in loaned capital, self-sustaining through repayments (the fund's management costs are covered by the returns)
  - Year 10: The fund has financed 100+ projects and generated a societal savings of SEK 500+ million

**Evaluation of the pilot**:

  - Measure the funds' "social return" (SROI – Social Return on Investment). Goal: every invested krona generates at least 3 kronor in societal savings.
  - Also measure systemic effects: Has the fund led public actors to start working more preventively? Have silos between administrations been broken down?
  - Compare with regions that do not have social investment funds (control group).

**Financing**: The start-up capital is taken from the Ministry of Health and Social Affairs' appropriation for "welfare innovation" (currently about SEK 500 million/year). The operating costs (SEK 10 million/year) are a fraction of the expected savings. If the funds generate an SROI of 3:1 (conservative), society saves SEK 450 million per year after a few years—an enormous societal return.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **New law on social investment funds** – defines the legal form (independent foundation with the purpose of financing preventive social interventions). Gives the funds the right to receive repayments from public actors (today there is no such mechanism—one municipality cannot "get back" money from another administration).
2.  **Amendment to the Local Government Act (2017:725)** – allows municipalities to enter into agreements on "social repayments" where savings are shared with an external fund.
3.  **Public Procurement Ordinance (LOU)** – exemption for social investment funds: projects financed by the fund do not need to be procured according to the LOU because they are "research and development" (exemption is already possible, but is clarified).

## Expected resistance and answers

### "This is privatizing welfare – private investors should not make money from people's misery."

**Answer**: The fund is a non-profit **foundation**. Any returns are reinvested in new preventive interventions—not distributed as dividends. Private donors can donate money, but do not receive interest or profit. The model is sometimes called a "social impact bond" or "pay for success"—and has been used successfully in several countries without eroding welfare.

### "It's impossible to measure social return – not everything can be reduced to numbers."

**Answer**: Most things can be measured if you are meticulous. Randomized control groups are the gold standard. But even simpler methods (matched comparisons, difference-in-differences) work. The important thing is to be transparent: report both quantitative and qualitative results. We already have investments based entirely on gut feeling—and they work poorly.

### "This will become a way for municipalities to push costs onto the state."

**Answer**: On the contrary—the municipalities match the state's financing (1:2) and take on a portion of the risk. And because the savings are shared (50% to the fund, 50% to the municipality/region), the municipalities have an incentive to participate actively. It is a win-win.

### "Are there any successful examples?"

**Answer**: Yes, several. The UK's "Social Impact Bond" (SIB) in Peterborough reduced recidivism in crime by 9% and gave investors a 3% return (and society large savings). The US's "Pay for Success" models have been used for everything from foster care placements to asthma treatment. Sweden so far only has a few small pilots (e.g., the "Uppsala model" for young adults). It is time to scale up.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **The Uppsala model (social investment funds for youth)** – a small pilot where private investors stepped in and received money back upon reduced benefit dependency. Worked technically but was too small.
  - **Health and Social Care College (Vård- och omsorgscollege)** – collaboration between municipalities and private actors to train staff. A pioneer in the partnership model.
  - **Almi's social loans** – Almi has lent money to social enterprises, but without the clear repayment connection to public savings.
  - **Denmark's "Sociale Investeringsfonde"** – Denmark has had a national social investment fund since 2018 with good results. Sweden can learn and do better.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a municipal or regional commissioner**: Write a letter of intent with two other municipalities/regions to jointly start a social investment fund. Ask for state co-financing (2:1). Use the text above as a basis.

**If you are an official in social services, the labor market, or public health**: Identify a preventive intervention that you already know works (but cannot get funding for). Calculate the savings. Send an idea to a potential future fund.

**If you are a philanthropist or entrepreneur**: Contact a region or municipality and offer to help start a social investment fund as a foundation—you can contribute start-up capital (donation) and/or board expertise.
