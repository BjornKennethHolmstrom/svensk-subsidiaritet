# Portfolio-based assessment

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Competence portfolios as an alternative or complement to traditional grades. Students document project work, skill demonstrations, peer reviews, and community contributions in a digital portfolio. The assessment is done by teachers together with external reviewers (from working life or higher education) and focuses on actual ability, not on test results. The goal: to measure what matters—creativity, problem-solving, collaboration, practical wisdom—which today's standardized grades miss. Three universities are piloting the portfolio as a basis for admission alongside merit rating.

## The mechanism

Today, almost all assessment in Swedish schools is based on written tests and assignments graded by the teacher. The grade is a synthesis of these results. The system optimizes for the ability to write tests under time pressure—not for the ability to solve real problems, collaborate in teams, or create something of value.

**The measure**: Introduce **portfolio-based assessment** as an equal path to grades and admission:

  - Every student builds a **digital portfolio** throughout their school years (from grade 7 to upper secondary school graduation).
  - The portfolio contains:
      - *Project work*: documentation of longer assignments (e.g., starting a mini-company, building a model, conducting an investigation).
      - *Skill demonstrations*: video clips, images, audio recordings where the student demonstrates a practical skill (e.g., giving an oral presentation, solving a technical task, cooking a meal).
      - *Peer reviews*: written assessments from other students about collaboration skills and contributions to group work.
      - *Community contributions*: documentation of involvement in associations, volunteer work, internships, entrepreneurship.
      - *Self-reflections*: students regularly write about their own learning, their strengths, and areas for development.
  - The assessment is made by:
      - **The teacher** (60% of the weight) – assesses subject knowledge and progression.
      - **External reviewers** (30%) – representatives from working life or higher education who assess relevance and quality.
      - **Peers** (10%) – assess collaboration and group dynamics (anonymized).
  - The portfolio is supplemented with **national tests** in the core subjects (to ensure equivalence and calibration), but the test result is just one factor among several.

**What the portfolio measures that grades miss**:

| Dimension | Grades | Portfolio |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Creativity | Hard to measure | Documented through projects and original solutions |
| Real-world problem solving | Abstract | Shown through practical demonstrations |
| Collaboration skills | Invisible | Peer reviews and group documentation |
| Perseverance | Not at all | Long projects show the ability to follow through |
| Communication skills | Written (test) | Oral, visual, written in different contexts |
| Ethical judgment | Not at all | Reflections on one's own and others' actions |

## Pilot design – 10 schools and 3 universities first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-4)**: 10 compulsory and upper secondary schools (varying in size, geography, socio-economics) test portfolio-based assessment instead of or alongside grades. Three universities (e.g., Uppsala, Lund, Umeå) commit to testing portfolios as a basis for admission to at least 5 educational programs.

**Selection criteria for schools**:

  - The school has a digital infrastructure (computers, platform) that enables portfolio work.
  - The teachers are interested and receive training (40 hours) in portfolio-based assessment.
  - The school commits to following the pilot's guidelines and participating in evaluation.

**Organizational model**:

  - Each school appoints a **portfolio coordinator** (0.2 FTE) responsible for implementation, technical support, and calibration between teachers.
  - The teachers in the pilot receive halved teaching time to manage assessment and tutoring (portfolios are more time-consuming than grading tests, but provide a better basis).
  - An **external reviewer pool** is established in collaboration with the Confederation of Swedish Enterprise (Svenskt Näringsliv), trade unions, and universities. The reviewers receive 40 hours of training and a fee per assessment.
  - A **digital platform** (open source) is developed to store, share, and assess portfolios. The platform supports video uploads, peer review, and external access.

**The admission process at universities**:

  - Applicants who want to use a portfolio instead of (or to complement) grades apply as usual via antagning.se.
  - The portfolio is assessed by two independent reviewers (from subject-relevant departments or working life) according to a transparent matrix.
  - Grades from national tests are used as calibration (to avoid portfolios from "easy" schools being unfairly favored).
  - After the pilot, an evaluation is made on whether portfolio-based students cope with the studies as well as or better than students admitted on grades.

**Evaluation**:

  - Compare students in pilot schools with matched control schools (traditional grades).
  - Efficacy measures: motivation (survey scale), attendance, completion rate, transition to work/studies.
  - The universities measure: dropout rate after the first semester, study results, perceived preparation.
  - Cost comparison: portfolio assessment vs. traditional tests and grades (teacher time, administration).

| Item | Amount (10 schools + 3 universities) |
|------|--------------------------------------|
| Platform (development, operation, support) | SEK 2,000,000 (one-time) |
| Teacher training (40 hrs × 100 teachers × SEK 500/hr) | SEK 2,000,000 (one-time) |
| Portfolio coordinators (10 × 0.2 FTE × SEK 500,000/year) | SEK 1,000,000/year |
| Reduced teaching time (100 teachers × 10 hrs/week × 35 weeks × SEK 400/hr) | SEK 14,000,000/year |
| External reviewer pool (fee per assessment, estimated 1,000 students × 2 reviewers × SEK 1,000) | SEK 2,000,000/year |
| Evaluation (researchers, data collection) | SEK 1,500,000/year |
| **Total first year** | **SEK 22.5 million** |
| **Annual operation after scale-up** | **SEK 18.5 million** (excl. one-time) |

**Financing**: SEK 22.5 million is a very small proportion of the school budget (300 billion). The funds can be taken from the Swedish National Agency for Education's development funds and state grants for digitalization. The universities' costs for admission (approximately SEK 50,000 per educational program) can be accommodated within their regular budgets.

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the School Ordinance (2011:185)** – add a section: *"Assessment of the student's knowledge may, in addition to tests and grades, take place through portfolio-based assessment in accordance with regulations from the National Agency for Education. The portfolio can form the basis for grading in one or more subjects."*
2.  **Amendment to the Higher Education Ordinance (1993:100)** – add: *"When admitting to basic education, a higher education institution may, in addition to grades and the Swedish Scholastic Aptitude Test (högskoleprovet), also use competence portfolios as a basis for assessment. The government may grant exceptions for pilot activities."*
3.  **The National Agency for Education's general guidelines** – a new section on portfolio assessment with guidelines for calibration, external review, and quality assurance.
4.  **GDPR adaptation** – portfolios contain personal data (image, video). Consent from the student/guardian is required, as well as technical solutions for storage and deletion. Simple templates are produced.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "Portfolios are subjective – grades are objective."

**Answer**: Grades are far from objective. Research shows great variation between teachers and schools (the same performance can get different grades). The portfolio reduces subjectivity through external review, peer assessment, and clear matrices. It is more legally secure than today's system.

### "It will be too much work for the teachers."

**Answer**: Portfolio assessment requires more time per student, but fewer tests and less grading. In the pilot, teachers get reduced teaching time (10 hrs/week) to compensate. If the portfolio leads to deeper learning and higher motivation, it may be worth the investment. The alternative is today's system with a 40% teacher flight—that is unsustainable.

### "Universities won't accept portfolios – they want numbers."

**Answer**: University admission is regulated in the Higher Education Ordinance, which currently only allows grades, the Swedish Scholastic Aptitude Test, and in some cases work samples or interviews. In order for portfolios to be used, a legislative amendment or a government decision on pilot exemptions is required. The pilot's purpose is precisely to create the basis needed to convince the government and universities that portfolios work. Research from other countries (e.g., USA, Canada) shows that portfolios can predict study success as well as or better than grades—but Swedish evidence is currently lacking.

### "Students from under-resourced homes will not benefit – they can't afford coaches who build nice portfolios."

**Answer**: The portfolio is built during school hours, using the school's digital tools and teacher guidance. External coaches are not allowed. Reviewers are trained to see through "polished surfaces" and assess actual knowledge. On the contrary, the portfolio can benefit students who are not good at taking tests but have other strengths—often from under-resourced homes.

### "Are there international examples?"

**Answer**: Yes. The **USA** has "portfolio-based assessment" in many schools and districts (e.g., New York Performance Standards Consortium). **Canada** (British Columbia) uses portfolios as part of the graduation exam. The **UK** has "coursework" which is similar to a portfolio, but often less comprehensive. **Finland** is testing portfolios in some schools. Sweden can become best in class.

## Swedish examples to build upon

  - **The upper secondary school diploma project (Gymnasiearbetet)** – already a form of portfolio, but only for one subject and often weakly integrated. The scale model exists.
  - **Digital student portfolios** (e.g., Schoolsoft, Google Classroom) – many schools already use digital tools to collect student work. Our model utilizes existing infrastructure.
  - **The Swedish Scholastic Aptitude Test (Högskoleprovet)** – a complement to grades. The portfolio becomes a third leg.
  - **Norway's "eksamen med mappevurdering" (exam with portfolio assessment)** – Norway has portfolio assessment in several subjects. Sweden can copy and improve.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a teacher or principal**: Test on a small scale—ask students to create a simple portfolio (e.g., in Google Drive) for a project. Have them include peer feedback and self-reflection. Use the portfolio as a basis for grades instead of a test. Evaluate how it felt.

**If you are a university teacher or director of studies**: Contact your department board and propose a pilot with portfolio admission to a program with high application pressure. Collaborate with an upper secondary school. Show that it's possible.

**If you are a student or parent**: Ask your school: "Can we use a portfolio instead of some tests? I would like to show what I know in other ways." The more people who ask, the harder it is to ignore.
