# A multicultural curriculum

[← Back to the main article](..)

**Summary**: Replace the narrow canon (Vikings, the Middle Ages, 19th-century literature) with portfolio-based cultural heritage education. Students in Tensta document their families' oral traditions *at the same time* as they learn about Swedish history. The goal: that every student sees their own and others' cultural heritage as a resource, not a problem. Today, 20% of students with a foreign background are marginalized—their families' cultures are made invisible in the curriculum.

## The mechanism

Today, the curriculum (Lgr22, Gy11) prescribes a "Swedish cultural heritage" that emphasizes:

  * Viking Age and Middle Ages (Vikings, runes, medieval ballads)
  * 19th-century literature (Strindberg, Almqvist, Lagerlöf, Topelius)
  * Christian tradition (the church year, hymns, Bible stories)
  * Swedish folk traditions (Midsummer, Lucia, miscellanea)

Content from other cultures is at best mentioned as "international perspectives" or "multicultural elements," but is not integrated into the core. Students with roots in Somalia, Iraq, Syria, Turkey, Chile, or Thailand get to learn a lot about Vikings but nothing about their parents' poetry, music, or history.

**The measure**: Introduce **portfolio-based cultural heritage education** where:

  * Each student builds a portfolio of their own cultural heritage during their time in school
  * The portfolio contains oral stories, recipes, songs, holidays, and crafts from the student's family or local environment
  * The teaching includes comparative perspectives: "Viking burial customs and Kurdish burial traditions—what is similar, what is different?"
  * National tests in history, Swedish, and civics always contain at least one assignment where the student must relate to a non-Swedish cultural heritage
  * School libraries and teaching materials reflect this diversity (at least 30% of the titles in other languages or about other cultures)

**What it enables**:

  * A Swedish-Somali student can write an essay about Somali oral poetry and compare it with Old Norse skalds
  * A group of students with a Kurdish background can do a project on Newroz celebrations and connect it to Swedish spring traditions
  * All students learn that cultural heritage is not a fixed list but living, changeable, and diverse

## Pilot design – ten schools first

**Pilot phase 1 (years 1-3)**: Ten compulsory and upper secondary schools in different municipalities test portfolio-based cultural heritage education. The schools receive extra resources for teacher training, purchasing teaching materials, and collaborating with local cultural associations.

### Pilot 1: Rinkeby School (Stockholm, grades F–9)

  * **Student composition**: 95% foreign-born or with both parents born outside Sweden. Largest language groups: Somali, Arabic, Turkish.
  * **Measure**: Introduce a subject called "Cultural heritage in practice" (2 hours/week) where students interview their parents and grandparents, record stories, and cook traditional dishes. Each semester, a portfolio (text, image, audio) is presented. The school collaborates with local associations (the Somali Cultural Association, the Turkish National Association).
  * **Budget**: SEK 500,000/year (teacher resources, technology, association compensations).

### Pilot 2: Vasa School (Kiruna, grades F–6)

  * **Student composition**: A mix of Swedish, Sami, and newly arrived (from Syria, Afghanistan).
  * **Measure**: Sami culture is integrated into all subjects (not just crafts and science). Students learn about Sami stories, reindeer husbandry, and duodji (crafts) alongside Swedish folk traditions. Newly arrived students contribute their stories about mountains and deserts—comparing living conditions.
  * **Budget**: SEK 300,000/year (Sami teaching materials, study visits, lecturers).

### Pilot 3: Lindeborg School (Malmö, grades 7–9)

  * **Student composition**: High proportion of students with a background in the former Yugoslavia, Iraq, Afghanistan.
  * **Measure**: Introduce a "history workshop" where students work with source criticism and oral history. Each student chooses an object, a story, or a tradition from their family and researches its origins, changes over time, and connection to Swedish history. Exhibition at the city library every semester.
  * **Budget**: SEK 400,000/year (research technology, exhibition, publication).

**Evaluation after 3 years** (compared to control schools):

  * Proportion of students who feel that the school "takes my background seriously" (survey)
  * Difference in test results in history/Swedish (expected to increase as students become more motivated)
  * Number of times teachers report conflicts between students from different backgrounds (expected to decrease)
  * Parents' participation in school activities (expected to increase)

## Regulatory changes required

1.  **Amendment to the curriculum (Lgr22, Gy11)** – add to the syllabus for history, Swedish, and civics: *"The student shall be given the opportunity to explore and present cultural heritage from different parts of the world, with a particular focus on the traditions represented in their own class and local environment."* And: *"National tests shall contain assignments that require comparison between at least two different cultural traditions."*
2.  **The Education Act's chapter on teaching materials (2010:800)** – add: *"One-third of school libraries and teaching materials shall consist of material in languages other than Swedish or that depicts non-Swedish cultural heritage."* Exemption for schools with an extremely homogeneous student composition (but they barely exist in today's Sweden).
3.  **The Higher Education Ordinance regarding teacher education** – introduce a mandatory course in "multicultural didactics" (7.5 credits) for all prospective teachers in history, Swedish, and civics.

## Expected resistance and answers

### "Swedish children must learn Swedish history first."

**Answer**: Yes, and they do. The Viking Age, the Swedish Empire, 19th-century literature—all of this remains. But it is complemented with other perspectives. A student who learns about Sami culture or Kurdish poetry does not become worse at Swedish history—they learn to see connections and comparisons, which is a higher cognitive level. And it is not either-or; it is both-and.

### "This is identity politics in school."

**Answer**: The current curriculum is also identity politics—it conveys a specific, narrow image of Swedishness. The question is what kind of identity politics we want: one that excludes 20% of the students or one that includes everyone? Highlighting the students' own backgrounds is not "political"—it is pedagogically sound. Research shows that students perform better when teaching connects to their experiences.

### "The teachers don't have the energy—they already have too much."

**Answer**: That is why there are pilots first and a gradual implementation. Portfolio-based teaching is not extra work if it replaces something else (e.g., a traditional assignment on Strindberg). And the teachers receive continuing education and ready-made models. Many teachers already work like this informally—we formalize and support it.

### "Parents with a Swedish background will protest—'my children shouldn't learn about Islam'."

**Answer**: Cultural heritage is not the same as religion. Learning about Kurdish Newroz or Somali poetry is about culture, not dogma. And if a parent protests against their child learning about *someone else's* cultural heritage—then we have an integration problem that rather confirms the need for the reform.

### "The national tests will be impossible to grade fairly."

**Answer**: On the contrary—they will become fairer. Today, students who can refer to Strindberg and the Viking Age are rewarded. Students from other backgrounds have to struggle to reach the same frame of reference. With assignments that require comparison, all students can use their own knowledge and be assessed on equal terms. Grading guidelines are adapted—there are already models for this in international tests (e.g., PISA's assessment of multicultural texts).

## Swedish examples to build upon

  * **Mångkulturellt centrum (Botkyrka)** – has developed material for intercultural teaching, but it is used sporadically. Could be scaled up.
  * **Sami teaching materials** – The Swedish National Agency for Education has already developed material on Sami history and culture. The same model can be used for other minorities.
  * **"Berättarministeriet" (The Storytelling Ministry)** – works with writing and oral storytelling in vulnerable areas. Their methodology (portfolio, interviews, publication) is a role model.
  * **Norway's "Cultural heritage in school"** – since 2020, the Norwegian curriculum has an explicit requirement that students must explore *their own and others'* cultural heritage. Sweden is lagging behind. Copy Norway.

## What you can do tomorrow

**If you are a teacher**: Test a small version—ask your students to interview a parent about a tradition and present it orally. Compare it with a Swedish tradition. See what happens with their engagement.

**If you are a principal**: Allocate SEK 50,000 to purchase teaching materials in other languages (e.g., books in Arabic, Kurdish, Somali) and start a "cultural heritage week" where students exhibit their portfolios.

**If you are a local school politician**: Submit a proposal to introduce portfolio-based cultural heritage education in a pilot project with 3–5 schools. Use the text above as a basis.
